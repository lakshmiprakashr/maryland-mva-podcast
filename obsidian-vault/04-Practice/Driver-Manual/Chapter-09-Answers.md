# Chapter 09 — Driver's Manual — Answer Key

## Answers with Explanations

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** B) By driving or attempting to drive on any Maryland road or public-use private property, you automatically consent to take a drug or alcohol test if a law enforcement officer requests it.

**Explanation:** Maryland's implied consent law means that the act of driving itself constitutes agreement to submit to testing. This applies to blood tests administered by a drug recognition expert when an officer has reason to believe you are impaired by drugs, alcohol, or a controlled substance.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often think implied consent only applies to alcohol tests — it covers drugs and controlled substances as well.

**Common Mistake:** Believing you can refuse a test without consequences — a certified refusal statement from an officer results in automatic license suspension.

### Question 3
**Correct Answer:** C) For the first 151 days, you may not drive unsupervised with passengers under 18 unless they are immediate family members.

**Explanation:** The passenger restriction limits new drivers from carrying teen passengers, which is a leading risk factor for new driver crashes. The restriction applies specifically to the first 151 days of holding a Provisional License.

**MVA Test Trap:** The number is 151 days, not 180 or 150 — this specific number is a common test target.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the restriction applies only to friends, not relatives — it covers all non-family passengers under 18.

### Question 9
**Correct Answer:** B) Suspension is a temporary loss of driving privilege; revocation is more severe — you cannot drive until the MVA determines you can safely drive again, and there is a minimum waiting period before you can request reinstatement.

**Explanation:** Suspension allows for a possible restricted license (for work/school), while revocation means no driving at all during the waiting period. The MVA can deny a reinstatement request even after the waiting period.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often confuse the two — suspension is temporary and may allow restricted driving, revocation is more serious with a mandatory waiting period.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking revocation has no waiting period or that you automatically get your license back after revocation.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 10

**Explanation:** While suspension is temporary and revocation allows for potential reinstatement, cancellation permanently ends your driving privilege. The MVA may cancel if you should not have been given a license, provided false information, or committed fraud.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think cancellation is less severe than revocation — it is actually the MOST severe of the three actions.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming you can apply for reinstatement after cancellation — you cannot.

### Question 17
**Correct Answer:** D) Suspension is the least severe — it is temporary, may allow a restricted license for work/school, and you can regain full driving privileges after the suspension period. Revocation is more severe — there is a minimum waiting period before you can request reinstatement, and the MVA can deny your request. Cancellation is the most severe — your driving privilege is permanently terminated and cannot be reinstated. The MVA may cancel if you obtained the license through fraud, provided false information, or should not have been given a license.

**Explanation:** These three actions form a hierarchy of severity. The key distinction is that suspension is temporary with possible restricted driving, revocation requires a waiting period and MVA approval for reinstatement, and cancellation is permanent with no reinstatement possible.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often rank revocation as the most severe — cancellation is actually the most severe because it is permanent and not reinstated.

**Common Mistake:** Not recognizing that all three require license surrender and that the period begins when the MVA receives your license.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 19
**Correct Answer:** D) Refusing a test triggers immediate administrative suspension through the MVA, based on the officer's certified refusal statement. This happens regardless of court outcomes. If you are later convicted in court, you face additional court penalties (fines, possible jail, etc.) AND the MVA imposes its own penalties (suspension, possible revocation). The administrative action and court penalties are independent — one does not replace the other. This is why Maryland law states that MVA penalties are imposed "in addition to" court penalties.

**Explanation:** The two systems — administrative (MVA) and judicial (court) — operate independently. You can face penalties from both. A refusal alone causes MVA suspension; a conviction causes court penalties plus additional MVA penalties.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think court penalties replace MVA penalties — they are cumulative.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming that a not-guilty court verdict reverses the administrative suspension — it does not automatically.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 20
**Correct Answer:** D) The wireless device restriction — which bans ALL wireless communication including hands-free for provisional and permit holders — reflects the graduated licensing philosophy that new drivers need fewer distractions while they develop driving skills. The MVA applies stricter rules to new drivers because: (1) novice drivers are statistically more crash-prone, (2) they have less experience managing multiple tasks while driving, and (3) research shows that any phone use (even hands-free) significantly impairs a new driver's attention. The wireless device ban is part of a broader set of restrictions — along with passenger, nighttime, and seat belt restrictions — designed to reduce risk during the most vulnerable period of driving.

**Explanation:** Graduated licensing systematically reduces risk factors for new drivers. The wireless device restriction is one of four key restrictions that together address the main causes of teen crashes: distraction (wireless device), peer pressure (passenger), fatigue/poor visibility (nighttime), and occupant protection (seat belt).

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think the wireless device restriction is arbitrary — it is based on crash data and distraction research.

**Common Mistake:** Not connecting the wireless device restriction to the other three provisional restrictions as part of a unified safety philosophy.

**Reference:** See chapter
