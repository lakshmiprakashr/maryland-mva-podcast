**Q18. [SYNTHESIS]** Compare the requirements for a supervising driver with the passenger restriction for provisional license holders. How do these two rules work together?

- B) The supervising driver must be 21+, licensed 3+ years, same vehicle class, and seated beside the driver. The passenger restriction prohibits provisional drivers under 18 from carrying non-family passengers under 18 for the first 151 days unless a qualified supervising driver is present. These rules work together: the supervising driver requirement allows provisional drivers to carry passengers who would otherwise be restricted, but only if the supervisor meets all qualifications. The passenger restriction ensures that when provisional drivers carry teen passengers, an experienced adult is present to guide them.

**Q19. [SYNTHESIS]** Explain how Maryland's bicycle laws parallel motor vehicle laws. What rights and responsibilities do bicyclists share with drivers?

- A) Varies by county
- B) Bicyclists have both rights and responsibilities on the road, similar to motor vehicle drivers. They must obey all traffic signals and signs, yield to pedestrians, ride on the right side of the road (with traffic, not against it), and signal all turns. They use appropriate lanes for their direction at intersections. The key difference is that bicyclists may also choose to dismount and cross as pedestrians for left turns. Bicyclists must also have specific equipment (lights, reflectors, brakes) similar to motor vehicle equipment requirements. Helmet requirements parallel child safety seat laws — both protect vulnerable road users based on age.

**Q20. [SYNTHESIS]** Compare the three classes of electric bicycles and electric low-speed vehicles. What distinguishes each type, and where do their capabilities overlap?

- A) Only required for commercial vehicles
- B) Electric bicycles all have human power with electric motor assistance, operable pedals, 2-3 wheels, and 750 watts or less. The three classes differ in speed and pedaling requirements: Class 1 (20 mph, pedaling only), Class 2 (20 mph, with or without pedaling), Class 3 (28 mph, pedaling only). Electric low-speed vehicles are different: operator only, under 100 pounds, handlebars with standing platform, and up to 20 mph. The overlap is that both electric bicycles and low-speed vehicles are powered by electric motors and human power, and both can reach 20 mph. The key difference is that e-bikes have pedals and seats, while low-speed vehicles have handlebars and standing platforms.
