# Chapter 01 — Driver's Manual — Answer Key

## Answers with Explanations

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** D) Vision screening, knowledge test, and driving skills test.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires every applicant to pass all three tests before a driver's license is issued. The MVA may also require additional requirements for some individuals.

**MVA Test Trap:** Answering with only two tests or including "written test" and "road test" without mentioning the vision screening, which is a separate requirement.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the vision screening is a distinct licensing requirement — it is not bundled with the knowledge test.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1 (page 7)

---

### Question 2
**Correct Answer:** C) 20/40 in each eye.

**Explanation:** The requirement is 20/40 acuity in EACH eye individually, not combined. Applicants must also have binocular vision and a continuous field of vision of at least 140 degrees.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the 20/40 requirement is for both eyes combined, or that it applies to one eye only.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing visual acuity (sharpness of vision) with field of vision (peripheral range).

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1.A (page 7)

---

### Question 3
**Correct Answer:** C) 25 questions, 20 minutes.

**Explanation:** The knowledge test covers motor vehicle laws, safe driving practices, and traffic signs. All content in the Driver's Manual is testable material.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing the numbers — thinking it is 20 questions in 25 minutes rather than the correct 25 questions in 20 minutes.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming the knowledge test is longer or has fewer questions than it actually does.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1.B (page 7)

---

### Question 4
**Correct Answer:** C) 140 degrees.

**Explanation:** This ensures drivers can see hazards approaching from the sides. The field of vision must be continuous, meaning no gaps in peripheral vision. This is measured separately from visual acuity.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing 140 degrees with other numbers in the manual, such as the 20/40 acuity standard.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the field of vision requirement is 180 degrees (full peripheral) when it is actually 140 degrees.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1.A (page 7)

---

### Question 5
**Correct Answer:** B) Type 1 Learner's Instructional Permit, valid for up to 2 years.

**Explanation:** First-time drivers who have never held a noncommercial driver's license must get a Type 1 Permit. It requires passing a vision screening and knowledge test.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing Type 1 with Type 2 — Type 2 is for drivers who already have a license and want a different class.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming there is only one type of Learner's Permit when Maryland has two distinct types.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2.B (page 9)

---

### Question 6
**Correct Answer:** A) Up to 180 days, and the applicant must be at least 16 years and 6 months old.

**Explanation:** Type 2 Permits are for drivers who already hold a license and want to change license classes. You can take the driving test for a full license after holding the permit for at least 14 days.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking Type 2 Permits last 2 years like Type 1, or that the minimum age is 15 years 9 months (that applies to Type 1).

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the 14-day minimum hold period with the 180-day maximum validity.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2.B (page 9)

---

### Question 7
**Correct Answer:** A) A noncommercial driver's license, also called a Class C license.

**Explanation:** Class C is the standard license most drivers need. It covers everyday personal vehicles. Commercial vehicles require different license classes.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking Class C is a commercial license, or that you need a special license for pickup trucks and vans.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing that "noncommercial" and "Class C" refer to the same license type.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2 (page 9)

---

### Question 8
**Correct Answer:** C) The step-by-step process for getting a noncommercial driver's license, progressing from Learner's Permit to Provisional License to full Driver's License.

**Explanation:** GLS applies to all first-time drivers of any eligible age. It is designed to give new drivers progressive driving experience before granting full driving privileges.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking GLS only applies to teen drivers — it applies to anyone who has never held a driver's license.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing GLS with a teen-only program rather than a universal licensing pathway.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2.A (page 9)

---

### Question 9
**Correct Answer:** A) Yes. If you are under 16, you must also provide a completed Learner's Permit School Attendance Certification form (DL-300).

**Explanation:** Maryland requires proof of school attendance for applicants under 16 to ensure they are meeting educational requirements before driving. The DL-300 form must be completed and submitted with the application.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking that a parent co-signature is the only requirement for minors, when the DL-300 form is also mandatory for those under 16.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming all minors need the DL-300 form — it is only required for applicants under 16, not those 16 to 17.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2.B (page 9)

---

### Question 10
**Correct Answer:** C) Your license will carry a corrective lens restriction, and you must wear your glasses or contacts every time you drive.

**Explanation:** If corrective lenses are needed to meet vision standards, the MVA places a restriction on your license. Driving without your required corrective lenses violates your license conditions and can result in a citation.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you only need to wear glasses during the vision screening, or that contacts do not count as corrective lenses.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming the restriction goes away over time — it remains on your license permanently unless your vision improves enough to pass without correction.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1.A (page 7)

---

### Question 11
**Correct Answer:** D) All of the following would prevent you from using the vehicle: cracked windshield, expired registration, no proof of insurance, less than half a tank of gas, or unsafe mechanical condition.

**Explanation:** The MVA requires the test vehicle to have up-to-date plates and registration, proof of insurance, be safe to drive, have a clean windshield, and at least half a tank of gas. Failure to meet any one of these disqualifies the vehicle.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking that a small windshield crack is acceptable, or that you can show proof of insurance on your phone rather than having the document.

**Common Mistake:** Focusing only on registration and insurance while forgetting the half-tank gas and windshield requirements.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1.C (page 8)

---

### Question 12
**Correct Answer:** A) No. Only you and the examiner giving the test are allowed in the vehicle during the driving skills test. An MVA-approved interpreter is the only additional person permitted if you need one.

**Explanation:** The MVA limits passengers during the test to ensure the examiner can focus on evaluating your driving. Your supervising driver must come to the test site but does not ride in the vehicle.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the supervising driver is allowed in the vehicle during the test, when they must come to the site but stay outside.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing who must come to the test (supervising driver) with who can be in the car during the test (only you and the examiner).

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1.C (page 8)

---

### Question 13
**Correct Answer:** C) Visit mva.maryland.gov and click "Make an Appointment," or call 410-768-7000 (or 1-800-492-4575 for TTY).

**Explanation:** The MVA offers online and phone scheduling. The TTY number is specifically for the deaf and hard of hearing. Walk-ins are not accepted for the driving skills test.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you can walk in for a driving skills test, or providing an incorrect phone number.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that an appointment is mandatory — you cannot simply show up for the driving skills test.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1.C (page 7)

### Question 14
**Correct Answer:** B) A document that proves your lawful status in the United States.

**Explanation:** Anyone born outside the U.S. must provide proof of lawful status. This is in addition to the standard requirements: age proof, SSN, and two Maryland residency documents.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the lawful status document replaces other required documents, when it is an additional requirement.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming a U.S. passport is sufficient for all purposes — you still need two separate documents proving Maryland residency.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2 (page 9)

### Question 15
**Correct Answer:** B) Yes. They need a completed DL-300 form (Learner's Permit School Attendance Certification) because the applicant is under 16.

**Explanation:** While the birth certificate proves age, SSN card proves Social Security, and utility bills prove residency, the DL-300 form is specifically required for applicants under 16 years old.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking that having the standard documents is enough, when age-specific requirements also apply.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing about the DL-300 form — many first-time applicants under 16 are surprised by this requirement.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2.B (page 9)

### Question 16
**Correct Answer:** B) Type 1 is for first-time drivers (never held a license), valid up to 2 years, requires passing vision and knowledge tests. Type 2 is for drivers changing license classes (already have a license), valid up to 180 days, requires minimum age 16 years 6 months, and you can take the road test after just 14 days.

**Explanation:** The two permit types serve different populations and have different timelines. Type 1 is designed for new drivers who need extended learning time. Type 2 is shorter because the applicant already has driving experience with a different license class.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking both permits have the same duration or the same eligibility requirements, or that Type 2 is just a renewal of Type 1.

**Common Mistake:** Not realizing that the 14-day minimum hold for Type 2 is much shorter than the typical holding period for Type 1.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2.B (pages 9-10)

---

### Question 17
**Correct Answer:** B) Before the test: vehicle must have valid registration, plates, and insurance. During the test: vehicle must be safe, have a clean windshield, half tank of gas, and only the examiner plus applicant inside. After the test: if you pass, you still need to meet ongoing requirements like reporting medical conditions.

**Explanation:** The vehicle requirements ensure both safety and fairness during evaluation. Pre-test requirements verify legal ownership and insurance. During-test requirements ensure the examiner can properly assess your skills without distractions or safety hazards.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking that meeting vehicle requirements once is sufficient — each test requires fresh verification of all conditions.

**Common Mistake:** Focusing only on the vehicle being "safe to drive" while forgetting specific items like windshield condition and gas level.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 1.C (pages 7-8)

---

### Question 18
**Correct Answer:** A) The knowledge test evaluates your understanding of motor vehicle laws, safe driving practices, and traffic signs — it is a written exam at the MVA. The driving skills test evaluates your ability to apply that knowledge on real roads and a test course. You must pass the knowledge test (and vision screening) first before taking the driving skills test.

**Explanation:** These tests build on each other. The knowledge test ensures you understand the rules. The skills test proves you can apply them. You cannot skip the knowledge test and go straight to the driving test — the GLS process requires sequential completion.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you can take the driving test first and study later, or that the knowledge test only covers traffic signs.

**Common Mistake:** Underestimating that the knowledge test covers three areas (laws, practices, signs) rather than just one.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Sections 1.B and 1.C (pages 7-8)

---

### Question 19
**Correct Answer:** D) At the MVA, you need age proof, SSN, lawful status (if applicable), and two Maryland residency documents. If under 18, a parent must co-sign. If under 16, you also need the DL-300 form. While holding the Permit, if you are under 25, you must complete the Driver Education Program. These requirements layer on top of each other based on your age and situation.

**Explanation:** Maryland uses an age-based tiered system. The core document requirements apply to everyone. Additional requirements (co-signature, DL-300, driver education) are added progressively based on how young the applicant is.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking that the same requirements apply to all applicants regardless of age, when age triggers additional obligations.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding that driver education is required while HOLDING the permit, not just before getting it.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Section 2 (pages 9-10)

---

### Question 20
**Correct Answer:** D) The vision screening sets the baseline: you must meet 20/40 acuity and 140-degree field of vision. If you need glasses or contacts, a restriction is added to your license. Separately, state law requires you to report any medical condition that could affect driving ability. These systems work together to ensure only safe drivers are licensed.

**Explanation:** Vision requirements are tested upfront. Medical conditions are self-reported and can be evaluated at any time. Both affect your eligibility to drive, but they are handled through different processes — vision is screened at the MVA, while medical conditions require proactive disclosure.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking that passing the vision screening means you never need to worry about vision-related restrictions, or that medical conditions are only checked at initial application.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding that the medical reporting requirement is ongoing and applies throughout the time you hold your license, not just at application.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Sections 1.A and 1.D (pages 7-8)
