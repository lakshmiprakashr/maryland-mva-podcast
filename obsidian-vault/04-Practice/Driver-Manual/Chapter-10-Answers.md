# Chapter 10 — Driver's Manual — Answer Key

## Answers with Explanations

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** C) The supervising driver must be at least 21 years old, currently licensed for at least three years in Maryland or another state to drive the same class of vehicles, and seated beside the permit holder (except for motorcycles).

**Explanation:** These requirements ensure the supervising driver has sufficient experience and maturity to guide a new driver. The three-year license requirement means they are not a newly licensed driver themselves.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often think the supervising driver must be 18 or 25 — the correct age is 21.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the supervising driver must be licensed for the same class of vehicles — a motorcycle license does not qualify someone to supervise a car driver.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 2
**Correct Answer:** A) All children under age 8 must ride in a child safety seat, unless the child is 4 feet 9 inches or taller.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires child safety seats for young children based on both age and height. The height exception recognizes that taller children may be safely restrained by standard seat belts.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often confuse the age (under 8) with the air bag rear seat age (under 13) — they are different rules.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the height exception is 5 feet — it is specifically 4 feet 9 inches.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 3
**Correct Answer:** C) You must notify the MVA when the condition is diagnosed. The MVA's Driver Wellness and Safety Division and/or Medical Advisory Board must approve you for a license.

**Explanation:** Medical conditions must be reported not only at license application and renewal but also when diagnosed after licensure. The MVA evaluates whether the condition could affect your ability to drive safely.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think you only need to report conditions when applying or renewing — you must also report upon diagnosis.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the MVA must approve you; it is not automatic.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 4
**Correct Answer:** C) Corrected minimum visual acuity of 20/70 in at least one eye, or a binocular field of vision of at least 110 degrees.

**Explanation:** These are the minimum vision standards. If an eye problem prevents you from meeting these standards, you must report it to the MVA, and the Medical Advisory Board must approve your license.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often think the standard is 20/20 — it is 20/70 corrected, which is much more lenient.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the single-eye acuity (20/70) with the binocular field requirement (110 degrees).

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 5
**Correct Answer:** C) It requires the MVA to allow eligible customers to apply to register to vote or update voting information when they get a driver's license or photo identification card.

**Explanation:** Also known as "Motor Voter," this federal law makes the MVA a voter registration point. It is designed to increase voter participation by combining license applications with voter registration.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may not know the common name "Motor Voter" — the test may use either name.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking you must register to vote at the MVA — it is optional, but the MVA must offer the opportunity.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 6
**Correct Answer:** B) A white beam headlight visible from 500 feet and a red rear reflector visible from 600 feet.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires specific lighting for nighttime bicycle riding. The headlight and reflector distances are different numbers — 500 feet for the headlight and 600 feet for the reflector.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often mix up the distances — headlight is 500 feet, reflector is 600 feet, not the same number for both.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the reflector distance (600 feet) is greater than the headlight distance (500 feet).

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 7
**Correct Answer:** A) Class 1: motor assists only when pedaling, stops at 20 mph. Class 2: motor assists whether or not pedaling, stops at 20 mph. Class 3: motor assists only when pedaling, stops at 28 mph.

**Explanation:** The classes differ in two ways: whether the motor requires pedaling (Classes 1 and 3 do, Class 2 does not) and the maximum assisted speed (Classes 1 and 2 at 20 mph, Class 3 at 28 mph).

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often think all e-bikes are the same — the class distinctions matter for where they can be ridden.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing Class 2 (always assists) with Class 3 (pedaling only but faster).

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 8
**Correct Answer:** B) Pull-in parking, back-in parking, and a three-point turn.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students still prepare heavily for parallel parking — it is no longer tested.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that pull-in and back-in parking are both required, not just one.

### Question 9
**Correct Answer:** D) A fine of up to $2,500 per vehicle per year.

**Explanation:** Maryland requires all registered motor vehicles to be insured by a Maryland-licensed company. The penalty for non-compliance is substantial — up to $2,500 per vehicle, per year, not a one-time fine.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think the fine is a flat amount — it is per vehicle and per year.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the insurance must be from a Maryland-licensed company, not just any company.

### Question 10
**Correct Answer:** A) The driver and ALL passengers of a motor vehicle must wear a safety belt or be buckled in a child safety seat at all times.

**Explanation:** Maryland's seat belt law covers everyone in the vehicle, not just the driver. There are no exceptions for back-seat passengers — everyone must be buckled up.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think seat belts are only required for front-seat occupants — Maryland requires them for all passengers.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the law applies to passengers, not just the driver.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 11
**Correct Answer:** B) No — the supervising driver must be at least 21 AND licensed for at least 3 years. Your friend is 22 (meets age) but only licensed for 2 years (does not meet the 3-year requirement).

**Explanation:** Both requirements must be met: age 21+ AND licensed for 3+ years. Meeting only one is not sufficient.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students focus on the age requirement and forget the 3-year licensing requirement.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming any adult can supervise — the 3-year license requirement is critical.

### Question 12
**Correct Answer:** A) No — a child in a rear-facing car seat should never ride in the front seat of a vehicle with a passenger air bag. This is both dangerous and violates Maryland guidelines.

**Explanation:** Passenger air bags deploy with tremendous force that can seriously injure or kill a child in a rear-facing seat. Maryland law and safety guidelines strictly prohibit this arrangement.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think air bags are always safe — they are dangerous for rear-facing child seats.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the air bag can be turned off — the rule is absolute regardless of air bag status.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 13
**Correct Answer:** B) Maintain steady pressure on the brake pedal. Do not pump the brakes or release the pedal. The vibration and rumble are normal ABS operation.

**Explanation:** When ABS engages, it rapidly pumps the brakes to prevent wheel lockup. The vibration and noise are designed feedback telling you the system is working. Pumping or releasing the pedal defeats the purpose of ABS.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think something is wrong and release the brake — this is the worst thing to do.

**Common Mistake:** Applying old driving knowledge of pumping brakes during a skid — with ABS, you maintain pressure.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 17
**Correct Answer:** B) The lap belt should be placed under the abdomen, as low as possible on the hips and across the upper thighs. The shoulder belt should be placed over the rounding of the belly.

**Explanation:** This positioning directs crash forces to the stronger pelvic bones rather than the abdomen, protecting both the mother and unborn child. The shoulder belt across the belly distributes force more safely.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think pregnant women should not wear seat belts — they absolutely should, with proper positioning.

**Common Mistake:** Placing the lap belt across the abdomen instead of below it on the hips.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 18
**Correct Answer:** D) The supervising driver must be 21+, licensed 3+ years, same vehicle class, and seated beside the driver. The passenger restriction prohibits provisional drivers under 18 from carrying non-family passengers under 18 for the first 151 days unless a qualified supervising driver is present. These rules work together: the supervising driver requirement allows provisional drivers to carry passengers who would otherwise be restricted, but only if the supervisor meets all qualifications. The passenger restriction ensures that when provisional drivers carry teen passengers, an experienced adult is present to guide them.

**Explanation:** The supervising driver rules and passenger restrictions are complementary safety measures. The passenger restriction limits risk by reducing teen passengers, while the supervising driver exception allows supervised practice with passengers.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think the two rules are independent — they directly interact when provisional drivers carry passengers.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the supervising driver exception overrides the passenger restriction only when the supervisor is present.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 19
**Correct Answer:** D) Bicyclists have both rights and responsibilities on the road, similar to motor vehicle drivers. They must obey all traffic signals and signs, yield to pedestrians, ride on the right side of the road (with traffic, not against it), and signal all turns. They use appropriate lanes for their direction at intersections. The key difference is that bicyclists may also choose to dismount and cross as pedestrians for left turns. Bicyclists must also have specific equipment (lights, reflectors, brakes) similar to motor vehicle equipment requirements. Helmet requirements parallel child safety seat laws — both protect vulnerable road users based on age.

**Explanation:** Maryland treats bicyclists as legitimate road users with corresponding duties. The rules of the road apply to bicycles just as they do to cars, with some accommodations for the different physical characteristics of bicycles.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think bicyclists follow different rules — they follow the same rules of the road with minor accommodations.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that bicyclists must signal turns and use appropriate lanes just like drivers.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 20
**Correct Answer:** B) Electric bicycles all have human power with electric motor assistance, operable pedals, 2-3 wheels, and 750 watts or less. The three classes differ in speed and pedaling requirements: Class 1 (20 mph, pedaling only), Class 2 (20 mph, with or without pedaling), Class 3 (28 mph, pedaling only). Electric low-speed vehicles are different: operator only, under 100 pounds, handlebars with standing platform, and up to 20 mph. The overlap is that both electric bicycles and low-speed vehicles are powered by electric motors and human power, and both can reach 20 mph. The key difference is that e-bikes have pedals and seats, while low-speed vehicles have handlebars and standing platforms.

**Explanation:** Maryland classifies these vehicles based on their design characteristics, power source, and maximum speed. Each class has different rules for where and how it can be used.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think all electric vehicles are classified the same way — the class distinctions matter for regulations.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the speed limits between Class 2 e-bikes (20 mph) and Class 3 e-bikes (28 mph).

**Reference:** See chapter
