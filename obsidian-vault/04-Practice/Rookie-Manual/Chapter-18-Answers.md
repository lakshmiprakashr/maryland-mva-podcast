# Chapter 18 — Rookie Manual — Answer Key

## Answers with Explanations

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** D) Two to three feet between vehicles.

**Explanation:** This gap provides enough room to maneuver into the space. Your front seat should be in line with the vehicle you are parking behind before you begin backing in.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you should be closer — being too close limits maneuvering room and increases the risk of contact.

**Common Mistake:** Not aligning the front seat with the other vehicle before starting the backing maneuver.

### Question 2
**Correct Answer:** B) No more than 12 inches from the curb.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires parallel-parked vehicles to be within 12 inches of the curb. This prevents the vehicle from protruding into the travel lane.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing the 12-inch curb distance with other parking measurements.

**Common Mistake:** Not checking the final curb distance after completing the park — always verify before exiting the vehicle.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 3

**MVA Test Trap:** Getting out of the car to meet the officer — this is potentially dangerous and should never be done.

**Common Mistake:** Reaching for your wallet or documents before the officer asks — keep your hands visible first.

### Question 5

### Question 10
**Correct Answer:** C) Stop immediately and yield to the pedestrian. Wait until they have safely passed before continuing.

**Explanation:** The manual states you must check traffic in all directions and yield to pedestrians, bicyclists, and other vehicles during parallel parking. Pedestrians always have the right of way.

### Question 12
**Correct Answer:** D) Back up very slowly while checking mirrors and looking over both shoulders. If visibility is severely limited, inch out gradually until you can see past the adjacent vehicles, then continue backing.

**Explanation:** Angle parking creates blind spots on both sides. Slow, incremental movement gives you time to react if a vehicle or pedestrian suddenly appears.

**MVA Test Trap:** Backing out quickly to "get it over with" — this is the most common cause of parking lot accidents.

**Common Mistake:** Only looking in one direction instead of checking both sides continuously.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 17
**Correct Answer:** A) Entering involves: signal, align parallel, 2-3 feet gap, brake, reverse, check traffic, back with sharp turn, turn opposite, adjust. Exiting involves: check traffic, signal, brake, reverse toward car behind, drive forward with turn toward travel direction, check front bumper, steer into traffic. The steps essentially reverse — entering goes back into the space, exiting pulls forward out of it, and both require checking all directions.

**Explanation:** The parallel parking maneuver is symmetrical — what you do going in is essentially the reverse of what you do going out. Both require signaling, checking traffic, and careful steering.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking exiting is just "drive away" — it requires the same careful checking and maneuvering as entering.

**Common Mistake:** Not treating exiting with the same care as entering — both are potentially dangerous maneuvers.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 18
**Correct Answer:** B) A moving violation leads to a conviction (or PBJ), which triggers three things: (1) the eighteen-month provisional waiting period restarts from zero, (2) mandatory attendance at the Driver Improvement Program, and (3) if you fail to attend the program, your license is suspended. This chain means one ticket can set back your driving privilege progression significantly.

**Explanation:** The Maryland GDL system has escalating consequences designed to encourage safe driving during the provisional period. Each consequence builds on the previous one.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking only the fine matters — the procedural consequences are far more significant for provisional drivers.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding that a PBJ carries the same GDL consequences as a full conviction.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 19

The citation consequences, Driver Improvement Program, and GDL requirements are all designed to reduce these numbers. **Explanation:** The manual uses data to motivate compliance — when you understand the real-world impact of unsafe driving, the rules feel less like arbitrary requirements and more like life-saving protections.

**MVA Test Trap:** Seeing the statistics as separate from the legal requirements — they are directly connected.

**Common Mistake:** Not recognizing that the GDL system exists because of these crash statistics.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 20
**Correct Answer:** B) Both require signaling intentions, moving slowly, checking for traffic, and centering the vehicle. Angle parking is simpler — you pull forward into the space and back out. Parallel parking is more complex — you must align parallel to another vehicle, back in with specific steering inputs, and end up within 12 inches of the curb. Exiting angle parking requires backing up; exiting parallel parking requires a combination of reversing and moving forward.

**Explanation:** Angle parking is a single-direction maneuver (pull in, back out). Parallel parking is a multi-direction maneuver that tests steering precision and spatial judgment.

**MVA Test Trap:** Treating parallel parking as just another form of angle parking — the techniques are fundamentally different.

**Common Mistake:** Not practicing both types because "I will only use one" — both are tested and both are encountered in daily driving.

**Reference:** See chapter
