# Chapter 13 — Rookie Manual — Answer Key

## Answers with Explanations

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** D) Identify, Predict, Decide, Execute.

**Explanation:** I.P.D.E. is the hazard perception system taught in Maryland. You identify risks, predict what might happen, decide on the best course of action, then execute that action.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often mix up the order of Predict and Decide. Remember: you predict BEFORE you decide.

**Common Mistake:** Reversing the order of steps, particularly Predict and Decide.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 2
**Correct Answer:** A) You yield to traffic already in the roundabout coming from your left.

**Explanation:** The roundabout operates as a one-way counter-clockwise flow. Entering traffic must yield to vehicles already circulating from the left.

**MVA Test Trap:** Many students instinctively think yield to the right as at a normal intersection. In a roundabout, you yield to the left.

**Common Mistake:** Yielding to the right instead of the left.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 3
**Correct Answer:** B) 3 to 4 seconds under ideal conditions.

**Explanation:** At highway speeds, 3 to 4 seconds gives you enough time to react and brake safely. This distance should increase in bad weather or at night.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may answer 2 seconds, which is the minimum for residential driving. Expressways require 3 to 4 seconds.

**Common Mistake:** Using the residential following distance instead of the expressway distance.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 4
**Correct Answer:** B) Accelerate to highway speed and signal to indicate you are merging into traffic.

**Explanation:** The entrance ramp is designed for acceleration. You must reach highway speed before merging to avoid creating a dangerous speed differential.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think you should slow down on the ramp. The opposite is true, you must accelerate.

**Common Mistake:** Entering the expressway too slowly, forcing other drivers to brake or swerve.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 5
**Correct Answer:** B) When approaching oncoming vehicles and when driving in fog.

**Explanation:** High beams reflected off fog actually reduce your visibility. High beams aimed at oncoming drivers can temporarily blind them. Switching to low beams prevents both hazards.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think high beams are always better for visibility. In fog and with oncoming traffic, they are worse.

**Common Mistake:** Keeping high beams on in fog, which causes light to reflect off water droplets and impair vision.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 6
**Correct Answer:** B) Identify the exit in advance, signal, check mirrors and perform head checks, use the deceleration lane if available, and adjust speed after exiting.

**Explanation:** Expressway exits require advance planning. Last-second exits cause dangerous lane changes. The deceleration lane allows you to slow down without slowing through traffic.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think you can brake in the travel lane before exiting. You should use the deceleration lane.

**Common Mistake:** Not signaling early enough or not using the deceleration lane.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 7
**Correct Answer:** D) 3 feet.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires a 3-foot space cushion when passing or driving near bicyclists, whether or not a bicycle lane exists.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think the rule only applies where bicycle lanes are present. It applies on all Maryland roads.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming the 3-foot rule is conditional on road markings.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 8
**Correct Answer:** B) Stop completely unless you are entering on a green signal.

**Explanation:** A complete stop ensures you have time to assess the high-speed traffic on the highway before merging. Only a green signal exempts you from stopping.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think a yield sign means they can roll through. You must stop completely at a stop sign.

**Common Mistake:** Rolling through a stop sign at a highway cross-street entry.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 9
**Correct Answer:** D) Accelerate on the ramp to match highway speed before merging. If you cannot reach highway speed, wait for a larger gap in traffic.

**Explanation:** Entering at a 20 mph speed differential is extremely dangerous. The ramp is designed for acceleration. Use it fully.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think they should merge immediately regardless of speed. Speed matching is critical.

**Common Mistake:** Merging too slowly and creating a hazard for drivers behind you.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 10
**Correct Answer:** B) Yield to the pedestrian first, wait for them to clear the crosswalk, then yield to traffic from your left before entering the roundabout.

**Explanation:** Pedestrians have the right of way at both entry and exit points of a roundabout. You must yield to them before anything else.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may focus only on vehicular traffic and forget about pedestrians at roundabouts.

**Common Mistake:** Not stopping for pedestrians at the roundabout entry crosswalk.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 11
**Correct Answer:** B) Stop completely at the stop line, signal left, check for oncoming traffic in both directions, select a large gap, then accelerate into the proper lane when safe.

**Explanation:** Entering a high-speed highway from a cross street requires a complete stop and careful gap selection. Rushing this maneuver is a leading cause of highway entry crashes.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may not realize they need to select a LARGE gap because highway traffic moves fast.

**Common Mistake:** Selecting a gap that is too small for highway speeds.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 12
**Correct Answer:** C) Switch to low beams and slow down. High beams reflect off fog droplets and actually make visibility worse.

**Explanation:** Low beams direct light below the fog line, reducing glare. You should also increase following distance and reduce speed.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think turning on high beams will help them see better in fog. It makes things worse.

**Common Mistake:** Using high beams in fog instead of switching to low beams.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 13
**Correct Answer:** D) Signal right, check mirrors and perform a head check, move into the right lane well before the exit, then use the exit ramp or deceleration lane.

**Explanation:** Identifying your exit early and moving to the right lane in advance prevents last-second lane changes across multiple lanes.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think they can cross all lanes at the last second. The manual says to identify exits in adequate time.

**Common Mistake:** Waiting too long to change lanes for an exit.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 14
**Correct Answer:** C) Pull over to a safe location and take a break. Night driving requires extra concentration and fatigue is a major crash risk.

**Explanation:** The Maryland manual specifically warns that night driving is tiring and recommends frequent breaks. Drowsy driving is as dangerous as impaired driving.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think they can push through fatigue. The manual explicitly recommends taking breaks.

**Common Mistake:** Trying to drive through fatigue instead of pulling over to rest.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 15
**Correct Answer:** C) On a weekend morning when traffic is light and weather conditions are good.

**Explanation:** Starting in ideal conditions lets the new driver build confidence and skills before facing heavy traffic or bad weather.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think practicing during rush hour is better for realism. The manual recommends light traffic first.

**Common Mistake:** Starting expressway practice in difficult conditions instead of ideal ones.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 16
**Correct Answer:** D) From an entrance ramp, you accelerate to highway speed on the ramp before merging. From a cross street, you must stop completely and then accelerate to highway speed after finding a gap.

**Explanation:** The entrance ramp provides a dedicated acceleration zone. A cross street does not, so you must stop first and then build speed after entering. Both require gap selection, but the starting points differ.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think both procedures are identical. The stop requirement at cross streets is the critical difference.

**Common Mistake:** Not stopping at a cross-street entry when no green signal is present.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 17
**Correct Answer:** C) In both situations, you identify the hazard (circulating traffic or merging traffic), predict what other drivers will do, decide on your action (yield or merge), then execute that action smoothly.

**Explanation:** I.P.D.E. is a universal framework. For roundabouts, you identify circulating traffic, predict their path, decide when to enter, then execute. For merging, you identify a gap, predict its closure, decide when to merge, then execute.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think I.P.D.E. only applies to intersections. It applies to all driving situations.

**Common Mistake:** Failing to apply I.P.D.E. to non-intersection scenarios like expressway merging.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 18
**Correct Answer:** A) Night driving introduces reduced visibility, glare from other vehicles, and increased fatigue. The 10-hour requirement ensures new drivers build experience managing these specific hazards before driving independently.

**Explanation:** The manual discusses high beam usage, fog hazards, fatigue, and glare. These are all night driving challenges. Ten hours of practice builds the judgment needed to handle them safely.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think 10 hours is arbitrary. It is a specific GDL requirement tied to crash data.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding why night driving is more dangerous and requires extra practice hours.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 19
**Correct Answer:** A) The deceleration lane allows you to reduce speed after leaving the travel lanes, so through traffic is not affected. Using it prevents rear-end collisions from drivers behind you who expect you to maintain highway speed in the travel lane.

**Explanation:** If you brake in the travel lane, drivers behind you at highway speed may not react in time. The deceleration lane isolates your slowing from the flow of traffic.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think the deceleration lane is optional. Using it is a critical safety practice.

**Common Mistake:** Braking in the travel lane before or during an exit maneuver.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 20
**Correct Answer:** C) At a traffic light, right-of-way is controlled by signals. At a roundabout, entering traffic yields to circulating traffic from the left. Roundabouts are safer because they eliminate head-on and left-turn crossing collisions, which are the deadliest crash types.

**Explanation:** Traditional intersections have multiple conflict points. Roundabouts reduce these by directing traffic in one direction. The yield rule eliminates the need for signals while maintaining flow.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think roundabouts and traffic circles are the same. Modern roundabouts use yield rules, not signals.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing old-style traffic circles with modern roundabouts.

**Reference:** See chapter
