# Chapter 15 — Rookie Manual — Answer Key

## Answers with Explanations

### Question 1
**Correct Answer:** B) Maintain a proper interval, select an adequate gap in oncoming traffic, signal your intentions, check mirrors and perform a head check, steer smoothly into the passing lane and accelerate, and return to the original lane when the entire front of the passed vehicle is visible in your rear-view mirror.

**Explanation:** Each step must be completed in order. Skipping any step increases the risk of a collision during the passing maneuver.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may forget the head check step or think signaling is optional. Both are required.

**Common Mistake:** Not checking mirrors and performing a head check before changing into the passing lane.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 2
**Correct Answer:** D) No. Even when passing, you are not allowed to exceed the posted speed limit.

**Explanation:** Maryland law is explicit. The speed limit applies at all times, including during passing maneuvers. Exceeding it is a violation regardless of the reason.

**MVA Test Trap:** Many students believe you can speed up to pass. You cannot. The speed limit is absolute.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking that the need to pass quickly justifies exceeding the speed limit.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 3
**Correct Answer:** A) When the entire front of the passed vehicle can be seen in your rear-view mirror.

**Explanation:** Seeing the entire front means you have fully cleared the passed vehicle. Returning too soon risks a side-swipe collision.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think seeing just the front bumper is enough. You must see the entire front of the vehicle.

**Common Mistake:** Returning to the original lane before fully clearing the passed vehicle.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 4
**Correct Answer:** D) Start counting when the vehicle ahead passes a stationary object such as a lamp post or road marker. Stop counting when you reach the same object. You should count 3 to 4 seconds under ideal conditions.

**Explanation:** This method gives you a practical way to measure your gap in seconds rather than feet or car lengths, which is more accurate at varying speeds.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think following distance is measured in car lengths. The manual teaches the stationary object counting method.

**Common Mistake:** Not using a stationary object and instead guessing the distance.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 5
**Correct Answer:** A) Maintain your speed and lane position, check for oncoming traffic that may present a hazard to the passing vehicle, and slow down if necessary to allow the passing vehicle to reenter the lane.

**Explanation:** Slowing down or maintaining your lane helps the passing vehicle complete the maneuver safely. Checking for oncoming traffic ensures you can warn the passing driver if needed.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think they should speed up when being passed. You should maintain speed and let them pass.

**Common Mistake:** Speeding up when being passed, which traps the passing vehicle in the oncoming lane longer.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 6
**Correct Answer:** C) When approaching oncoming vehicles within 500 feet, when following another vehicle within 200 feet, and when driving in fog.

**Explanation:** High beams can blind oncoming drivers and create glare when following another vehicle. In fog, high beams reflect off water droplets and reduce visibility.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think high beams are always better. They must be dimmed in specific situations.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting to dim high beams when oncoming traffic approaches.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 7
**Correct Answer:** A) Interior lights reduce your ability to see outside by causing your eyes to adjust to the bright interior rather than the dark road ahead.

**Explanation:** Your pupils constrict in bright light, making it harder to see in the dark. The manual advises pulling over safely if you need to check something inside the vehicle.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think interior lights help them see inside the car. They actually impair outside vision.

**Common Mistake:** Turning on the dome light to read a map or check something while driving.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 8
**Correct Answer:** A) A minimum of 10 hours of supervised night driving practice.

**Explanation:** Night driving requires different skills than daytime driving. The GDL system ensures new drivers build experience before driving independently at night.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may not know the specific number. It is exactly 10 hours.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing the exact GDL night driving hour requirement.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 9
**Correct Answer:** B) Wait until the oncoming car passes and you have an adequate gap before initiating the passing maneuver.

**Explanation:** Passing requires a clear gap in oncoming traffic. If a car is approaching, the gap is not adequate and you must wait.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think they can squeeze past before the oncoming car arrives. This is extremely dangerous.

**Common Mistake:** Misjudging the gap and passing when oncoming traffic is too close.

**Reference:** See chapter

**Common Mistake:** Returning to the lane before fully clearing the passed vehicle.

### Question 11
**Correct Answer:** D) Maintain your speed and lane position. You may slow down if necessary to allow the passing vehicle to reenter the lane before the oncoming car arrives.

**Explanation:** Slowing down helps the passing vehicle complete the maneuver safely. The oncoming car is a hazard to the passing vehicle, not to you if you stay in your lane.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think they should speed up to prevent the pass. You should maintain speed and help them complete it safely.

**Common Mistake:** Speeding up and trapping the passing vehicle in the oncoming lane.

### Question 12
**Correct Answer:** C) Increase your following distance beyond the normal 3 to 4 seconds, use low beam headlights, and reduce your speed if necessary.

**Explanation:** Rain reduces traction and visibility. Night rain is especially dangerous. Increasing following distance gives you more time to react.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may maintain normal following distance and speed. Bad weather and night require adjustments.

**Common Mistake:** Not increasing following distance in rain at night.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 13
**Correct Answer:** B) Switch to low beams immediately. High beams reflect off fog droplets and create glare that reduces visibility.

**Explanation:** Low beams direct light below the fog line, reducing reflection. You should also reduce speed and increase following distance.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think switching to high beams will help. It makes fog visibility worse.

**Common Mistake:** Using high beams in fog instead of switching to low beams.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 14
**Correct Answer:** C) Pull over to a safe location on the side of the road before using your interior light or checking anything inside the vehicle.

**Explanation:** Interior lights reduce your ability to see the dark road ahead. The manual specifically advises pulling over first.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think turning on the dome light briefly is fine. The manual advises against any interior light use while driving.

**Common Mistake:** Turning on the interior light while driving instead of pulling over.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 15

### Question 16
**Correct Answer:** D) Pull over to a safe location and take a break. Do not try to push through fatigue.

**Explanation:** Night driving requires extra concentration. Fatigue impairs reaction time and judgment. The manual recommends frequent breaks to prevent fatigue.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think they can drive through mild fatigue. The manual explicitly recommends stopping.

**Common Mistake:** Trying to drive through fatigue instead of pulling over to rest.

**Reference:** See chapter

### Question 17
**Correct Answer:** D) When passing, you must maintain interval, select gap, signal, check, accelerate, and return only when the entire front is visible. When being passed, you maintain speed and lane position, may slow down to help, and check for hazards to the passing vehicle.

**Explanation:** The passing driver has primary responsibility for executing the maneuver safely. The driver being passed has a supporting role of maintaining predictability and helping when needed.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think both drivers have equal responsibility. The passing driver bears more responsibility.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding that being passed also requires active awareness and cooperation.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 18
**Correct Answer:** A) At night, visibility is reduced. You cannot see hazards as early. The manual says to increase following distance beyond the normal 3 to 4 seconds to compensate for reduced reaction time.

**Explanation:** Following distance is not fixed. It must be adjusted based on conditions. Night driving is one of the most important conditions requiring increased following distance.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think 3 to 4 seconds is always sufficient. The manual says to increase it at night.

**Common Mistake:** Not adjusting following distance for night driving conditions.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 19
**Correct Answer:** B) Exceeding the speed limit during passing reduces the time available to complete the maneuver safely and increases the severity of any collision that occurs. The speed limit is set for the road's design and conditions.

**Explanation:** Passing already involves higher risk. Adding excessive speed compounds that risk. The speed limit ensures you maintain control and can stop or swerve if needed.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think there is an exception for passing. There is not. The speed limit is absolute.

**Common Mistake:** Believing that passing justifies exceeding the speed limit.

**Reference:** See chapter

---

### Question 20
**Correct Answer:** A) Many teen crashes occur at night because new drivers fail to make necessary adjustments like slowing down, increasing following distance, and using proper beam settings. The 10-hour requirement builds the experience needed to recognize and manage these hazards.

**Explanation:** Night driving is statistically more dangerous for teens. The GDL requirement ensures supervised practice before independent night driving. The adjustments discussed in the chapter are the specific skills teens need to develop.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may not connect the GDL requirement to the specific hazards discussed. The connection is direct.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding why the 10-hour requirement exists and what skills it builds.

**Reference:** See chapter
