# Chapter 20 - Rapid Recall Drill

## Questions

**Q1. [Type: RECALL]** What is the Blood Alcohol Content threshold for Driving Under the Influence for drivers 21 and over?

**Answer:** .08 or above.

**Explanation:** A BAC of .08 or higher is legally considered DUI for drivers age 21 and over in Maryland. This is the standard threshold used nationwide.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing the DUI threshold with the DWI threshold — DWI is .07 to .079, DUI is .08 and above.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking DWI and DUI are interchangeable terms with the same BAC threshold.

---

**Q2. [Type: RECALL]** What is the BAC range for Driving While Impaired for drivers 21 and over?

**Answer:** .07 to .079.

**Explanation:** Maryland distinguishes between DWI and DUI. DWI covers the BAC range of .07 to .079, which is below the DUI threshold but still legally impaired.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking DWI is just a lesser form of DUI — they are separate charges with different BAC ranges.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing there are two distinct impaired driving charges in Maryland.

---

**Q3. [Type: RECALL]** What is Maryland's alcohol policy for drivers under 21?

**Answer:** Zero tolerance. You can receive a citation for consuming even one drink and driving.

**Explanation:** Maryland has zero tolerance for alcohol use by drivers under 21. Any detectable alcohol while driving results in a citation, regardless of BAC level.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the .08 BAC threshold applies to drivers under 21 — zero tolerance means any amount.

**Common Mistake:** Believing that a small amount of alcohol is legal for drivers under 21.

---

**Q4. [Type: RECALL]** What is Implied Consent in Maryland?

**Answer:** By signing and accepting your Learner's Instructional Permit, Provisional License, or full Driver's License, you agree to be tested for the presence of drugs and/or alcohol when requested by a law enforcement official.

**Explanation:** Implied Consent means that by obtaining any Maryland driver's license, you have already consented to chemical testing. Refusing to comply may result in the loss of your driving privilege.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking Implied Consent only applies to full license holders — it applies to permits and provisional licenses as well.

**Common Mistake:** Believing you have the right to refuse a breathalyzer without consequences.

---

**Q5. [Type: RECALL]** Can a DUI, DWI, or Refusal to Test be expunged from your driving record?

**Answer:** No, DUI, DWI, and Refusal to Test cannot be expunged from your driving record.

**Explanation:** These are permanent entries on your Maryland driving record. Unlike some other violations, they cannot be removed or sealed.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking that completing a program or waiting a certain period will clear these from your record.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming all driving record entries can eventually be expunged.

---

**Q6. [Type: RECALL]** What are the six situations where you must always contact law enforcement?

**Answer:** Someone has been injured, a vehicle cannot be moved, a driver appears to be under the influence, a driver does not have a license, a driver tries to leave the scene without giving proper information, or public property has been damaged.

**Explanation:** These situations require official intervention for medical, legal, or documentation purposes that go beyond what drivers can handle privately.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking only injury crashes require law enforcement — five other situations also require it.

**Common Mistake:** Trying to handle these situations privately.

---

**Q7. [Type: RECALL]** What happens if you refuse to comply with a law enforcement request for drug or alcohol testing?

**Answer:** Refusing to comply may result in the loss of your driving privilege.

**Explanation:** Implied Consent means you agreed to testing by accepting your Maryland license. Refusing violates this agreement and carries its own penalty separate from any DUI or DWI charge.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking refusal has no consequences — it can result in license loss even without a DUI conviction.

**Common Mistake:** Believing that refusing a test prevents a DUI charge — the refusal itself is a separate penalty.

---

**Q8. [Type: RECALL]** What must you do if you are in a crash where someone is injured?

**Answer:** Immediately contact 911 and stay at the scene until help arrives. Do not move vehicles.

**Explanation:** Injured persons may have serious injuries that could be worsened by movement. Emergency responders need the scene preserved to assess and treat properly.

**MVA Test Trap:** Trying to move injured people or vehicles — this can cause additional harm.

**Common Mistake:** Leaving the scene before emergency responders arrive.

---

**Q9. [Type: RECALL]** What are the consequences of both DUI and DWI charges in Maryland?

**Answer:** Both are extremely serious charges that may result in losing your privilege to drive for an extended period.

**Explanation:** The Maryland Driver's Manual emphasizes that both charges carry severe penalties including potential license revocation, fines, and criminal record entries that cannot be expunged.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking DWI is "no big deal" compared to DUI — both carry serious consequences.

**Common Mistake:** Underestimating the long-term impact of an impaired driving conviction.

---

**Q10. [Type: APPLICATION]** You are 19 years old and have one drink at a family gathering. You feel fine to drive. Are you legally allowed to drive?

**Answer:** No. Maryland has zero tolerance for alcohol for drivers under 21. You can receive a citation for consuming one drink and driving, regardless of how you feel.

**Explanation:** Zero tolerance means any detectable alcohol while driving results in a citation for drivers under 21. Feeling "fine" is irrelevant — the legal standard is any consumption.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking one drink is okay because you are "almost 21" — zero tolerance applies to all drivers under 21.

**Common Mistake:** Believing that being below the .08 BAC threshold is legal for under-21 drivers.

---

**Q11. [Type: APPLICATION]** You witness a crash where one driver appears to be intoxicated. What should you do?

**Answer:** Contact law enforcement immediately. The manual lists a driver appearing to be under the influence as one of the six situations requiring mandatory law enforcement contact.

**Explanation:** Impaired driving is a criminal offense. Law enforcement needs to respond to test the driver and prevent them from driving while impaired.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you should only report if someone is injured — DUI alone requires law enforcement.

**Common Mistake:** Not reporting because "someone else will call."

---

**Q12. [Type: APPLICATION]** You are pulled over and the officer asks you to take a breathalyzer test. You are nervous and want to refuse. What are the consequences of refusal?

**Answer:** Under Maryland's Implied Consent law, refusing to comply may result in the loss of your driving privilege. This is a separate penalty from any DUI or DWI charge.

**Explanation:** By accepting your Maryland license, you already consented to testing. Refusal does not prevent a DUI charge but adds its own penalty — potential license revocation.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking refusal is a safe option to avoid a DUI — it is not and carries its own consequences.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding that refusal is worse than failing the test because it combines penalties.

---

**Q13. [Type: APPLICATION]** You are in a minor fender bender where no one is hurt and both cars can move. The other driver does not have a license. What should you do?

**Answer:** Contact law enforcement immediately. The manual lists a driver not having a license as one of the six situations requiring mandatory law enforcement contact, even for minor crashes.

**Explanation:** An unlicensed driver is a legal issue that requires law enforcement documentation. This is separate from the crash itself.

**MVA Test Trap:** Handling it privately because "nobody is hurt" — the unlicensed driver situation always requires law enforcement.

**Common Mistake:** Letting the unlicensed driver leave without getting their information.

---

**Q14. [Type: APPLICATION]** You strike an unattended car in a parking lot and cannot find the owner. You leave a note with your name and phone number on the windshield. Is this sufficient?

**Answer:** Yes, this meets the requirement. The manual states you must attempt to find the owner and, if unable, leave your contact information in a secure place. You should also notify your insurance company of the incident.

**Explanation:** Leaving a note with your contact information is the standard approach for hitting an unattended vehicle. The note should be secure so it does not blow away.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you only need to find the owner — if you cannot, leaving your information is the required alternative.

**Common Mistake:** Not notifying your insurance company after the incident.

---

**Q15. [Type: APPLICATION]** A friend who is 22 years old tells you they drove home safely after having three beers. They say their BAC was probably under .08. Are they in the clear?

**Answer:** Not necessarily. If their BAC was between .07 and .079, they could be charged with DWI even if below the DUI threshold. Maryland has two separate impaired driving charges.

**Explanation:** Many people only know about the .08 DUI threshold and do not realize that Maryland also has a DWI charge at .07. Your friend could still face serious charges.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking .08 is the only number that matters — .07 is also a legal threshold in Maryland.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing about the DWI threshold and assuming anything below .08 is legal.

---

**Q16. [Type: APPLICATION]** You are in a crash and the other driver tries to leave without providing information. What should you do?

**Answer:** Contact law enforcement immediately. The manual lists a driver trying to leave the scene without giving proper information as one of the six situations requiring mandatory law enforcement contact.

**Explanation:** Leaving without providing information is a hit-and-run offense. Law enforcement needs to be contacted to identify and locate the other driver.

**MVA Test Trap:** Trying to stop the other driver yourself — contact law enforcement instead.

**Common Mistake:** Not getting the other vehicle's license plate number before they leave.

**Q17. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Explain the difference between DUI and DWI in Maryland, including the BAC thresholds, who they apply to, and the consequences of each.

**Answer:** DWI (Driving While Impaired) applies to drivers 21 and over with a BAC of .07 to .079. DUI (Driving Under the Influence) applies to drivers 21 and over with a BAC of .08 or above. Both are extremely serious charges that may result in loss of driving privilege for an extended period. For drivers under 21, zero tolerance applies — any alcohol while driving results in a citation regardless of BAC. Both DUI and DWI, along with Refusal to Test, cannot be expunged from your driving record.

**Explanation:** Maryland's tiered system means there are actually three levels of impaired driving consequences based on age and BAC: zero tolerance (under 21), DWI (.07-.079), and DUI (.08+).

**MVA Test Trap:** Treating DUI and DWI as the same charge — they have different BAC thresholds and are distinct legal charges.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing about the DWI threshold at .07.

---

**Q18. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Compare the three crash scenarios in the manual (injuries, immovable vehicle, movable vehicle) and explain why each has a different response protocol.

**Answer:** Injury crashes require 911 and scene preservation because medical emergencies take priority and evidence must be preserved. Crashes with immovable vehicles require 911 for traffic safety and coordination. Crashes with movable vehicles and no injuries are handled by drivers because the situation does not require emergency services — just proper documentation. The severity of the situation determines who handles the response.

**Explanation:** The manual's response protocol matches the level of official intervention needed. More severe situations get more official involvement.

**MVA Test Trap:** Using the same response for all crash types — each scenario requires a different approach.

**Common Mistake:** Not moving movable vehicles out of the travel lane after a minor crash.

---

**Q19. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Explain the relationship between Implied Consent, the right to refuse testing, and the consequences of refusal. Why does Maryland structure the law this way?

**Answer:** Implied Consent means by accepting any Maryland license, you agree to chemical testing when requested. You technically can refuse, but refusal carries its own penalty — potential license loss. This is separate from any DUI or DWI charge. Maryland structures it this way because impaired driving is a leading cause of fatal crashes. By creating consequences for refusal, the law ensures that drivers cannot avoid accountability simply by declining the test.

**Explanation:** The law balances your theoretical right to refuse with practical consequences that discourage refusal. This maximizes the likelihood of detecting impaired drivers.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking refusal is a loophole to avoid DUI charges — it is not and adds penalties.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding that refusal is a separate legal consequence, not a way to avoid the DUI charge.

---

**Q20. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Why does the Maryland Driver's Manual include impaired driving laws in a chapter about crashes? What is the connection between these topics?

**Answer:** Impaired driving is one of the six situations requiring mandatory law enforcement contact at a crash scene. If you witness a crash and the other driver appears impaired, you must call 911. Additionally, impaired driving is a leading cause of crashes. The manual connects the cause (impairment) with the effect (crashes) and the legal response (reporting requirements). Understanding both topics together gives you a complete picture of your legal responsibilities.

**Explanation:** The manual groups these topics because they overlap — impaired driving causes crashes, and crash reporting procedures must account for impaired drivers at the scene.

**MVA Test Trap:** Seeing these as unrelated topics — they are directly connected through crash causation and reporting requirements.

**Common Mistake:** Not recognizing that witnessing an impaired driver at a crash scene triggers mandatory law enforcement contact.

## Quick Reference

- **Under 21:** Zero tolerance for alcohol
- **DWI (21+):** BAC .07 to .079
- **DUI (21+):** BAC .08 or above
- **Implied Consent:** Any MD license = consent to testing
- **Refusal:** May result in license loss; separate from DUI/DWI
- **Cannot be expunged:** DUI, DWI, and Refusal to Test
- **Call 911 for six situations:** Injuries, immovable vehicle, DUI, no license, hit-and-run, public property damage
- **Injury crash:** Call 911, stay at scene, do not move vehicles
- **No injuries, can move:** Move out of travel lane, collect info
- **Info to collect:** Name, address, phone, insurance, plate, make/model, witnesses
- **Domestic animal strike:** Notify law enforcement
- **Unattended property:** Find owner or leave contact info, notify insurance

**Test Day Tip:** The three BAC numbers to memorize cold are: zero (under 21), .07 (DWI threshold), and .08 (DUI threshold). These appear on nearly every Maryland knowledge test.
