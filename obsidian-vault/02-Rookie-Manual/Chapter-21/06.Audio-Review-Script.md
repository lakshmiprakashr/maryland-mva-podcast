## [1:30] Section 1: Impaired Driving Laws

Let us start with the most important Maryland impaired driving laws. If you are under twenty-one, Maryland has **zero tolerance** for alcohol. That means even one drink while driving can result in a citation. You are not legally old enough to drink, so any alcohol in your system while driving is a violation.

[PAUSE]

Now, if you are twenty-one or older, there are two levels of impaired driving charges. The first is **Driving While Impaired**, or DWI. This applies when your BAC, which stands for blood alcohol content, is between point zero seven and point zero seven nine. Think of it as the lower threshold of impairment.

[PAUSE]

The second level is **Driving Under the Influence**, or DUI. This applies when your BAC is point zero eight or above. DUI is the more serious charge with more severe penalties.

[PAUSE]

Here is a memory trick. The numbers are point zero seven and point zero eight. Seven comes first, and DWI is the lesser charge. Eight comes second, and DUI is the more serious charge. Seven for DWI, eight for DUI. Remember that.

[PAUSE]

Both DUI and DWI are extremely serious charges that can result in you losing your privilege to drive for an extended period. These convictions **cannot be expunged** from your driving record. They stay permanently.

## [5:00] Section 2: Implied Consent and Practice Requirements

Now let us talk about **Implied Consent**. By signing and accepting your Learner's Instructional Permit, Provisional License, or full Driver's License, you also agree to be tested for the presence of drugs and alcohol when requested by a law enforcement official.

[PAUSE]

This is called Implied Consent. It means that by driving on Maryland roads with a Maryland license, you have already consented to chemical testing. Refusing to comply may result in the loss of your driving privilege.

[PAUSE]

Here is what the MVA exam loves to ask. Implied Consent applies to **all** license types, not just full licenses. If you have a Learner's Permit, a Provisional License, or a full Driver's License, Implied Consent applies to you.

[PAUSE]

Now, let us cover the practice driving requirements. If you are under twenty-five, you need a minimum of **sixty hours** of supervised practice, and at least **ten of those hours** must be at night. If you are twenty-five or older, you need **fourteen hours** total, with **three hours** at night.

[PAUSE]

Remember, these practice hours are **in addition to** the six hours of behind-the-wheel instruction you receive during your required driver education course. They are completely separate requirements.

[PAUSE]

Here is a memory trick. Think "under twenty-five, you need more time." Sixty hours is a lot, but that is because younger drivers benefit from more supervised experience. Think "twenty-five and up, you need less but still need some." Fourteen hours for older drivers.

[PAUSE]

One more important point. The sixty hours is described as a **suggested minimum** in the manual. You may always practice more, and the manual says more practice leads to safer driving.

## [8:30] Section 3: Test Strategy

The MVA exam loves to test the difference between DWI and DUI thresholds. Remember, point zero seven is DWI, point zero eight is DUI. If you see a question about BAC thresholds, check the age group first. Under twenty-one is zero tolerance, not point zero eight.

[PAUSE]

Another common test question is about Implied Consent. The exam may ask what happens if you refuse a breathalyzer test. The answer is that you may lose your driving privilege. Refusal is not a way to avoid charges.

[PAUSE]

For practice hour questions, always identify the driver's age first. Under twenty-five means sixty hours with ten at night. Twenty-five and older means fourteen hours with three at night. The exam will try to trick you by swapping these numbers.

[PAUSE]

The exam may also ask whether practice hours include driver education hours. They do not. Practice hours are always in addition to the six hours from driver education.
