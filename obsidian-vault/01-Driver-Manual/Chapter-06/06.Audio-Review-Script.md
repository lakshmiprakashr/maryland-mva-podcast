## [1:00 - 4:00] Impaired Driving: Alcohol, Drugs, and CDS

Let's start with impaired driving. Maryland's legal blood alcohol concentration limit is point oh eight. That's the number you absolutely need to memorize.

[PAUSE]

But here's what many people get wrong. Even though the legal limit is point oh eight, ANY amount of alcohol can affect your ability to drive safely. Your judgment and physical coordination can be impaired at a much lower level, and you could still face criminal charges.

[PAUSE]

Think about this. It is very difficult to know your own BAC or how alcohol is affecting you. Many things affect how your body processes alcohol. Your weight, body fat, when you last ate, how much you ate, how tired you are, your mood, and even medications can all change how alcohol affects you. You cannot simply count your drinks and know you are safe.

[PAUSE]

Now, if you are under twenty-one years old, Maryland has zero tolerance. That means no alcohol at all. If law enforcement stops you and you have been drinking, your license will be suspended or revoked. You could also face criminal charges for violating the alcohol restriction on your license.

[PAUSE]

The MVA exam loves to test the open container law. An open container is any open can, bottle, other type of container, or package. Here is the trick question. A six-pack of alcohol with open, empty, or missing bottles or cans can be considered an open container. It does not have to be currently open. Missing pieces count.

[PAUSE]

The passenger area includes the driver and passenger seats, plus any place the driver or passengers can easily reach from where they are sitting. So putting it in the back seat does not solve the problem.

[PAUSE]

Now let's talk about drugs. Controlled dangerous substances, or CDS, include illegal drugs, some prescription medications, and chemical inhalants. All of these can severely affect your alertness, concentration, coordination, and reaction time.

[PAUSE]

Here is what catches people off guard. If you drive after taking a medication that tells you not to drive, and it impairs your ability, you could be charged with a criminal offense. Always read your prescription and over-the-counter drug labels for warnings about driving.

[PAUSE]

And one more critical fact. If you are convicted of a drunk or drugged driving offense with a child in your vehicle, the judge can double your fine AND your jail time. Both penalties are doubled, not just one.

[PAUSE]

Remember the Maryland slogan. If you plan to drink, plan not to drive.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [4:00 - 7:30] Aggressive Driving and Distracted Driving

Now let's cover aggressive driving and distracted driving. These are two separate but related topics on the MVA test.

[PAUSE]

Aggressive driving includes a wide range of behaviors. Speeding, tailgating, ignoring traffic signals and devices, making unsafe lane changes, not yielding the right-of-way, and unsafe or illegal passing. The causes include crowded roads, unexpected delays, rushing, road construction, and stress.

[PAUSE]

The tips to reduce aggressive driving are straightforward. Allow extra time to travel. Be patient. Be courteous. Focus on your own driving behavior. Always signal your intentions. Obey all traffic laws, signs, signals, and pavement markings. Yield the right-of-way. And avoid competing with other drivers.

[PAUSE]

A good general rule is to treat other drivers and road users like you would like to be treated.

[PAUSE]

Now, distracted driving. This is when you take your eyes, hands, or especially your mind away from driving. Distracted driving causes more crashes that are reported to the police than anything else. One-third of fatal crashes are caused by distracted driving.

[PAUSE]

Many activities count as distracted driving. Eating and drinking. Adjusting the radio. Programming a GPS. Dealing with children and pets. Loose objects moving in the vehicle. Talking and texting on a cell phone. Smoking. Putting on makeup. Shaving. Reading. And interacting with others in the vehicle.

[PAUSE]

The cell phone laws are a major test topic. It is illegal to use a hand-held cell phone, including texting, while driving. You cannot use your phone while holding it. You can use it for calls while driving if you are not holding it, which is called hands-free. But even then, use it as little as possible.

[PAUSE]

And here is the critical age distinction. If you are under eighteen years old, you may not use a cell phone while driving, even hands-free. The only exception is a nine-one-one emergency call.

[PAUSE]

The MVA exam loves to ask about this difference. Adults can use hands-free. Drivers under eighteen cannot use any cell phone at all while driving.

[PAUSE]

Be the focused driver who avoids distractions and sticks to the task at hand: driving.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [7:30 - 10:30] Drowsy Driving and Emergency Vehicles

Our next topic is drowsy driving. Not getting enough sleep can cause unsafe driving. Sleepiness slows reaction time, decreases awareness, and impairs judgment, just like drugs or alcohol.

[PAUSE]

The danger signs for drowsy drivers are important for the test. Your eyes close or go out of focus. You have trouble keeping your head up. You cannot stop yawning. You have wandering, disconnected thoughts. You do not remember driving the last few miles. You missed your exit. You keep driving out of your lane. You keep changing your speed.

[PAUSE]

If you become tired or sleepy while driving, it is best to rest or change drivers. Being tired dulls your mind and slows down your reactions, making driving very dangerous.

[PAUSE]

Some drivers have a higher risk. People who drive many miles each day, people with sleep disorders, and people taking certain medications.

[PAUSE]

Now, let's quickly review the emergency vehicle rules, which overlap with this chapter.

[PAUSE]

When you see or hear an emergency vehicle approaching with its signals on, you must do three things immediately. One, move as close as possible to the edge of the road. Two, stay out of intersections. Three, remain stopped until the emergency vehicle has passed.

[PAUSE]

If you are going in the same direction as an emergency vehicle using its signals, you may not pass it unless it stops or a police officer tells you to pass.

[PAUSE]

And if you are approaching an emergency vehicle, tow truck, or other service vehicle that is stopped on the road, you must move to a lane that is not immediately adjacent to the stopped vehicle. If it is not possible to move over, you must slow down to a safe speed and prepare to stop if necessary.

[PAUSE]

On the Maryland knowledge test, remember that the Move Over Law applies to emergency vehicles, tow trucks, and service vehicles with flashing lights.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [10:30 - 12:00] Quick Review

Let's do a rapid recap of the must-know facts from Chapter Six.

[PAUSE]

Maryland BAC limit: point oh eight. Under twenty-one: zero tolerance, no alcohol at all. Open container: any open can, bottle, or six-pack with missing pieces in the passenger area.

[PAUSE]

Driving with a child during an impaired offense: judge can double both fine and jail time.

[PAUSE]

Aggressive driving examples: speeding, tailgating, ignoring signals, unsafe lane changes, not yielding, unsafe passing.

[PAUSE]

Distracted driving: eyes, hands, or mind off driving. Causes more police-reported crashes than anything else.

[PAUSE]

Cell phones: hand-held illegal for all drivers. Under eighteen: no phone use at all while driving, even hands-free, except nine-one-one.

[PAUSE]

Drowsy driving: impairs like alcohol. Signs include eyes closing, head dropping, yawning, wandering thoughts, forgetting miles driven, missing exits, lane drifting, speed changes.

[PAUSE]

Emergency vehicles: pull to edge, stay out of intersections, remain stopped until passed. Move over or slow down for stopped emergency and service vehicles.
