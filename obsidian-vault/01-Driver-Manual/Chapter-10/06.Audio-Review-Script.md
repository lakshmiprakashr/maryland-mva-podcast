## [1:30 - 5:00] Medical Conditions, Supervising Drivers, and Organ Donation

First up, medical conditions. If you have certain medical conditions, you must notify the MVA. And this is important: you must notify them when you apply for your license, when you renew it, and also if the condition is diagnosed after you already have your license.

[PAUSE]

The MVA's Driver Wellness and Safety Division, and sometimes the Medical Advisory Board, must approve your license if you have one of these conditions.

[PAUSE]

The conditions include diabetes that has caused low blood sugar episodes requiring help from another person in the past six months, epilepsy or seizures, heart conditions that caused loss of consciousness in the past six months, stroke, dizzy spells, fainting, or blackouts, sleep apnea or narcolepsy, a history of traumatic brain injury, weakness or numbness in your limbs, absent or amputated limbs, eye problems that prevent corrected vision of 20/70 in at least one eye, or a binocular field of vision under 110 degrees, alcohol or drug use problems, mental health conditions, schizophrenia, and dementia.

[PAUSE]

That is a long list, but the key point is this: if you have any condition that could affect your ability to drive, you must tell the MVA. And they must approve you.

[PAUSE]

Now, let us talk about supervising drivers. If you have a Maryland Learner's Permit, you must have a supervising driver with you. Here are the three requirements.

[PAUSE]

The supervising driver must be at least 21 years old. They must be currently licensed for at least three years in Maryland or another state. And they must be licensed to drive the same class of vehicles that you are driving. They must also be seated beside you, unless you are riding a motorcycle.

[PAUSE]

The MVA test often asks about these requirements. Remember: 21 years old, three years licensed, same class of vehicles, seated beside you. Those are the four things.

[PAUSE]

Next, organ donation. Anyone 18 years old or older can be an organ donor. Minors who are at least 16 can also be organ donors, but they need a parent or guardian to consent in writing. Your information goes into the Maryland Organ Donor Registry, managed by the Maryland Department of Health.

[PAUSE]

And here is something many people do not know about: you can also register to vote at the MVA. The National Voter Registration Act of 1993, often called Motor Voter, is a federal law that requires the MVA to let eligible customers register to vote or update their voting information when they get a driver's license or photo ID card.

[PAUSE]

So the MVA is not just about driving. It is also a place to handle organ donation and voter registration.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [5:00 - 8:30] Insurance, Seat Belts, and Child Safety Seats

Let us move on to insurance. Every motor vehicle registered in Maryland must be insured by a company licensed in Maryland. You must carry insurance for personal injury and property damage liability in the amounts required by law.

[PAUSE]

You must always have a valid insurance identification card with you when driving. Here is the good news: this card can be in electronic format. So your phone is fine. But if a law enforcement officer asks for it, you must show it. If you refuse, you could be fined.

[PAUSE]

And listen to this: if you do not keep insurance on all your vehicles, you can be fined up to 2,500 dollars per vehicle per year. That is not a one-time fine. That is per vehicle, per year.

[PAUSE]

Now, seat belts. This is simple but important. By law, the driver and ALL passengers of a motor vehicle must wear a safety belt or be buckled in a child safety seat. Everyone. Front seat, back seat, driver, passengers. Everyone.

[PAUSE]

Speaking of child safety seats, here are the rules. All children under age 8 must ride in a child safety seat, unless the child is four feet nine inches or taller. That height exception catches a lot of people on the test.

[PAUSE]

Children younger than two must ride in a rear-facing child safety seat that meets federal guidelines. And they must stay rear-facing until they reach the height or weight specified by the manufacturer. You cannot just flip them forward at a certain age. You have to follow the manufacturer's instructions.

[PAUSE]

If you cannot afford a child safety seat, you can contact Kids In Safety Seats, or KISS, at the Maryland Department of Health. Their number is 800-370-SEAT. They coordinate programs that rent car seats or booster seats at low cost.

[PAUSE]

Now let us talk about air bags. Air bags are important safety devices. For the best protection, use them with your lap and shoulder belt. Sit as far back from the steering wheel as possible. Try to keep 10 to 12 inches between the steering wheel and your chest.

[PAUSE]

For pregnant women, place the lap part of the seat belt under the abdomen, as low as possible on the hips, and across the upper thighs. Place the shoulder belt over the rounding of the belly.

[PAUSE]

Here is a critical safety rule: children in a rear-facing car seat should NEVER ride in the front seat of a vehicle with a passenger air bag. Never. And children under age 13 should ride buckled up in a rear seat, either in a child safety seat or with a seat belt.

[PAUSE]

So remember: under two means rear-facing. Under eight means child safety seat, unless four feet nine inches or taller. Under 13 means rear seat. Three different ages, three different rules.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [8:30 - 12:30] ABS, Headsets, Driving Test, and Bicycle Rules

Now let us cover some important equipment and test topics.

[PAUSE]

First, anti-lock braking systems, or ABS. ABS helps prevent your wheels from locking so you can control your car. When ABS engages, you may hear a rumble from the brakes and the brake pedal will vibrate under your foot.

[PAUSE]

Here is what the MVA test really wants you to know: that vibration and rumble are completely normal. Do not release the brake. Do not pump the brake. Maintain steady pressure. The system is working exactly as it should.

[PAUSE]

Next, headsets and earphones. You may not drive with earplugs, headsets, or earphones in both ears. Hearing aid devices are allowed, but not earphones or headsets.

[PAUSE]

Now, the driving test. Here is something that surprises many students: parallel parking is no longer required on the Maryland driving test. However, you will still learn it in the MVA driving education course. The skills are covered by the reverse two-point turn.

[PAUSE]

What IS required on the test are three maneuvers: pull-in parking, back-in parking, and a three-point turn. The parking is done in a 10 by 20 foot space, and the turn is done in a 16 by 20 foot space.

[PAUSE]

These maneuvers test your visual skills, space judgment, mirror use, turn signals, steering, braking, and acceleration control. So practice these three moves before test day.

[PAUSE]

Now, bicyclists. Like motor vehicle drivers, bicyclists have both rights and responsibilities on the road. They must obey the rules of the road, ride straight and single file, yield to pedestrians, and obey all traffic signals and signs.

[PAUSE]

Bicyclists must ride on the right side with traffic, signal all turns, and look back before changing lanes. For left turns, they can either turn like motor vehicles by moving to the left side of the travel lane, or they can get off the bicycle and walk across in a crosswalk.

[PAUSE]

Helmets are required for anyone under age 16 riding on a bicycle. This includes passengers, not just the person steering.

[PAUSE]

At night, Maryland law requires a white headlight on the front visible from 500 feet, and a red reflector on the back visible from 600 feet. Those are different numbers: 500 for the light, 600 for the reflector.

[PAUSE]

All bicycles must have brakes that can stop the bicycle within 15 feet when it is going 10 miles per hour on dry, level, clean pavement.

[PAUSE]

Finally, electric vehicles. Electric bicycles have human power with electric motor assistance, fully operable pedals, two or three wheels, and a motor rated at 750 watts or less.

[PAUSE]

There are three classes. Class 1 provides motor assistance only when pedaling, stopping at 20 miles per hour. Class 2 provides assistance whether or not you are pedaling, stopping at 20 miles per hour. Class 3 provides assistance only when pedaling, but stops at 28 miles per hour.

[PAUSE]

Electric low-speed vehicles are different. They are designed for the operator only, weigh less than 100 pounds, have handlebars and a standing platform, and can reach up to 20 miles per hour.

[PAUSE]

Note: if you use a low-speed electric vehicle, check for local restrictions in your area.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [12:30 - 14:00] Quick Review

Let us do a quick review of the key facts from this chapter.

[PAUSE]

Medical conditions: report to MVA at application, renewal, or upon diagnosis. The MVA must approve your license.

[PAUSE]

Supervising driver: 21 years old, three years licensed, same class, seated beside you.

[PAUSE]

Organ donor: 18 and older can donate; 16 and older with parental consent.

[PAUSE]

Insurance: Maryland-licensed company. Card required, electronic is fine. No insurance means up to 2,500 dollars per vehicle per year.

[PAUSE]

Seat belts: driver and all passengers.

[PAUSE]

Child safety seats: under 8, unless four feet nine inches tall. Under 2 means rear-facing. Under 13 means rear seat.

[PAUSE]

Air bags: 10 to 12 inches from the wheel. Rear-facing car seats never in front with passenger air bag.

[PAUSE]

ABS: pedal vibration is normal. Maintain pressure. Do not pump.

[PAUSE]

Headsets: not in both ears. Hearing aids are fine.

[PAUSE]

Driving test: no parallel parking. Required: pull-in parking, back-in parking, three-point turn.

[PAUSE]

Bicycles: helmets under 16. Headlight 500 feet. Reflector 600 feet. Brakes stop within 15 feet at 10 miles per hour.

[PAUSE]

Electric bicycles: Class 1 and 2 at 20 miles per hour. Class 3 at 28 miles per hour.
