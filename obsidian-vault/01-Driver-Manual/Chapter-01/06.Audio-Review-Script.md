## [1:30] Section 1: The Three Licensing Requirements

Here's the most important thing to remember from this entire chapter. To get a Maryland driver's license, you must pass three tests. Say it with me: vision screening, knowledge test, and driving skills test.

[PAUSE]

That's three. Not two. Not one. Three separate requirements. The Maryland Department of Transportation Motor Vehicle Administration — or M-V-A, as most people call it — requires all three before you can legally drive any motor vehicle. And when we say motor vehicle, we mean cars, vans, and pick-up trucks. If it has a motor and you're driving it on public roads, you need a license.

[PAUSE]

Now, the M-V-A may also ask some people to meet additional requirements. That's usually based on medical history or other factors. But the three core tests apply to everyone.

[PAUSE]

## [3:00] Section 2: Vision Screening and the Knowledge Test

The vision screening tests how well you can see. And here are the three numbers you absolutely must remember.

[PAUSE]

First: binocular vision. You need to be able to use both eyes together.

[PAUSE]

Second: visual acuity of at least twenty-forty in each eye. That's twenty-forty, not twenty-twenty. And here's the key — it's in EACH eye, not combined. So your left eye needs to see twenty-forty, and your right eye needs to see twenty-forty. You can't have one eye at twenty-twenty and the other at twenty-eighty and call it good.

[PAUSE]

Third: a continuous field of vision of at least one hundred forty degrees. That means your peripheral vision needs to cover at least one hundred forty degrees without any gaps.

[PAUSE]

Now, here's a common question: can your eye doctor do the screening instead of the M-V-A? Yes! The M-V-A or your eye doctor can perform the vision screening. So if you're already at the optometrist, you can get your screening done right there.

[PAUSE]

But what if you need glasses or contacts to meet these requirements? Then your license will have a corrective lens restriction. That means every single time you drive, you must wear your glasses or contacts. If you don't, you're driving without a valid license. Your license will say so right on it.

[PAUSE]

Alright, that's vision. Let's move on to the knowledge test.

[PAUSE]

The knowledge test covers three things: motor vehicle laws, safe driving practices, and traffic signs. All the information in this manual is testable. So study the whole thing, not just one section.

[PAUSE]

Here are two numbers to burn into your memory: twenty-five questions, twenty minutes. That's twenty-five questions in twenty minutes. Not twenty questions in twenty-five minutes. Twenty-five in twenty.

[PAUSE]

You take the knowledge test at an M-V-A location. And if you need special accommodations, check Section Two-A of the manual. The M-V-A also has an Online Driver Test Tutorial on their website with practice questions. Use it. It's free and it helps.

[PAUSE]

Now, let's talk about the driving skills test.

## [6:00] Section 3: The Driving Skills Test and Vehicle Requirements

The driving skills test is where you prove you can actually drive. It has two parts: driving on public roads and a special course at the testing location.

[PAUSE]

You must make an appointment. You cannot walk in for a driving skills test. To schedule, visit mva.maryland.gov and click "Make an Appointment," or call four-one-zero, seven-six-eight, seven-thousand. That's four-one-zero, seven-six-eight, seven-zero-zero-zero. For the deaf and hard of hearing, the TTY number is one-eight-hundred, four-nine-two, four-five-seven-five.

[PAUSE]

Now here's something that trips people up. You bring your own vehicle to the test. The M-V-A does not provide a car for you. And your vehicle must meet ALL of these requirements.

[PAUSE]

One: up-to-date license plates and registration. Two: proof that the vehicle is insured. Three: the vehicle must be safe to drive. Four: no cracks or other obstructions on the windshield that could affect your ability to see. And five: at least a half tank of gas.

[PAUSE]

That last one catches a lot of people. A half tank of gas. If you show up with a quarter tank, you may not be allowed to take the test. So check your gas gauge the night before.

[PAUSE]

Now, who can be in the vehicle during the test? Only three people. You, the examiner giving you the test, and if you need one, an interpreter approved by the M-V-A. That's it. Your friends cannot ride along. Your parent cannot ride along. Even your supervising driver — the person who had to come with you to the test site — stays outside the vehicle during the test.

[PAUSE]

And here's a health tip from the M-V-A: they encourage you to keep air flowing in the vehicle during the test. Open the windows. You can wear a mask if you want. And if you're not feeling well, reschedule. Don't risk it.

[PAUSE]

Before we move on, let me repeat the vehicle requirements one more time because the M-V-A exam loves to ask about these.

[PAUSE]

Up-to-date plates. Proof of insurance. Safe to drive. Clean windshield. Half tank of gas. Five things. Remember all five.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [8:30] Section 4: Documents, Permits, and the Graduated Licensing System

Now let's talk about what you need to bring to the M-V-A when you apply for your license.

[PAUSE]

You need four things. First: a document that proves your age and includes your full name. Your birth certificate or passport works. Second: if you were born outside the United States, you need a document proving your lawful status. Third: your Social Security Number, or proof that you're not eligible for one. And fourth: two documents that prove you live in Maryland. Two separate documents.

[PAUSE]

And here's the rule that trips people up: you must bring original documents or copies certified by the agency that provided them. The M-V-A will not accept photocopies. They will not accept notarized copies. They will not accept documents with any changes or erasures. Originals or certified copies only.

[PAUSE]

Now, let's talk about the Graduated Licensing System. This is called G-L-S, which stands for Graduated Licensing System. It's the step-by-step process for getting your license if you've never had one before. And here's what's important: it applies to people of ALL ages. Not just teens. If you've never had a driver's license, you go through the G-L-S.

[PAUSE]

The system starts with a Learner's Instructional Permit. Then you get a Provisional License. Then you graduate to a full Driver's License. Each step gives you more driving experience and more freedom.

[PAUSE]

There are two types of Learner's Permits. Type One is for first-time drivers. It lasts up to two years. You need to pass the vision screening and knowledge test to get it.

[PAUSE]

Type Two is for drivers who already have a license and want a different class. It lasts up to one hundred eighty days. You must be at least sixteen years and six months old. And here's the shortcut: you can take the road test for a full license after holding a Type Two Permit for just fourteen days.

[PAUSE]

So remember: Type One, two years, first-time drivers. Type Two, one hundred eighty days, changing license classes, sixteen and a half years old minimum.

[PAUSE]

Now, if you're under twenty-five, there are additional requirements. You must be at least fifteen years and nine months old to apply. If you're under eighteen, your parent or legal guardian must co-sign the application. If you're under sixteen, you need a completed DL-300 form — that's the Learner's Permit School Attendance Certification form. And while you hold your permit, if you're under twenty-five, you must complete the Maryland Driver Education Program.

[PAUSE]

That's a lot of rules. Let me repeat the age-based requirements. Fifteen-nine is the minimum age. Under eighteen, parent co-signs. Under sixteen, DL-300 form. Under twenty-five, driver education. Those four age milestones are fair game on the knowledge test.

- **Class C** — noncommercial driver's license
- **binocular vision** — using both eyes together
- **visual acuity** — sharpness or clarity of vision
- **field of vision** — the total area you can see without moving your eyes
- **corrective lens restriction** — a license condition requiring glasses or contacts
- **Graduated Licensing System** — the step-by-step process for new drivers
- **supervising driver** — a qualified adult who accompanies permit holders
