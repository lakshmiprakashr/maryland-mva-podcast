## [1:30 - 5:30] Provisional License Restrictions

Let us start with the four restrictions that apply to provisional license holders under 18 years old.

[PAUSE]

First: the Seat Belt Restriction. If you have a Provisional License, you must always wear a safety belt. And every single passenger in your car must also wear a safety belt or be in a child safety seat. No exceptions.

[PAUSE]

Second: the Passenger Restriction. This one trips up a lot of people on the test. If you are under 18 with a Provisional License, you may not drive unsupervised with passengers under 18 for the first 151 days. That is one-five-one. Not 150. Not 180. One hundred and fifty-one days.

[PAUSE]

The exception is immediate family members. And here is where the MVA test gets tricky. Immediate family means your spouse, daughter, son, stepdaughter, stepson, sister, brother, stepsister, stepbrother, or any relative who lives at your address. That is a very broad definition.

[PAUSE]

You can also drive with a non-family passenger under 18 if you have a qualified supervising driver with you. A supervising driver must be at least 21, licensed for at least 3 years, and seated beside you.

[PAUSE]

Third: the Nighttime Restriction. You cannot drive unsupervised between midnight and 5:00 a.m. unless you are going to or from one of four things: your job, an organized volunteer program, an official school activity, or an athletic event or related training session.

[PAUSE]

Let me repeat those four exceptions: work, volunteer, school activity, and athletics. Hanging out with friends does not count. Going to a party does not count. Only those four specific purposes.

[PAUSE]

Fourth: the Wireless Device Restriction. This is a big one. If you have a Provisional License or a Learner's Permit, you may not use ANY wireless communication device while driving. That includes hands-free phones. The only exception is a 911 emergency call.

[PAUSE]

The MVA exam loves to ask about this one. Students think hands-free is legal for everyone. It is not. For provisional and permit holders, even hands-free is banned.

[PAUSE]

So to recap the four restrictions: seat belt, passenger for 151 days, nighttime from midnight to 5 a.m., and wireless device ban including hands-free.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [5:30 - 8:00] Implied Consent and False Identification

Now let us talk about implied consent and what happens when you break the rules.

[PAUSE]

Implied consent means exactly what it sounds like. By driving in Maryland, you automatically agree to take a drug or alcohol test if a law enforcement officer asks you to. You do not have to sign anything. The act of driving itself is your consent.

[PAUSE]

If an officer believes you are impaired by drugs, alcohol, or a controlled substance, they can have a drug recognition expert request a blood test. Your license will be suspended if your blood alcohol concentration, or BAC, is .08 percent or higher.

[PAUSE]

And here is the key point: if you refuse the test, your license will be suspended anyway. The officer submits a certified statement that you refused, and the MVA suspends your license based on that refusal alone.

[PAUSE]

So refusing does not help you. It actually makes things worse because you lose your license AND you still face possible criminal charges.

[PAUSE]

Now, what about fake IDs? If you try to get a fake identification card, Learner's Instructional Permit, or driver's license, you may be fined and sent to prison under both federal and state law. And your driver's license may be suspended on top of that.

[PAUSE]

Lying about your age to buy alcohol carries the same penalties. So the consequences go far beyond just getting in trouble at a store.

[PAUSE]

The MVA test will ask about implied consent. Remember: driving equals consent, .08 BAC triggers suspension, and refusal also triggers suspension.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [8:00 - 11:30] Administrative Actions: Suspension, Revocation, and Cancellation

This section is where the MVA test really focuses. You need to know the three types of administrative actions and how they differ.

[PAUSE]

The MVA can take four types of actions: suspend, revoke, refuse, or cancel your license. When they do, they must mail you a notice telling you what action they plan to take, why, and what steps you can take to challenge it.

[PAUSE]

In most cases, you can request an administrative hearing. But no matter what, you must surrender your license to the MVA. You cannot keep it and just not drive.

[PAUSE]

First: Suspension. This is a temporary loss of your driving privilege. In some cases, the MVA may give you a restricted license that lets you drive only for specific purposes, like going to work or school.

[PAUSE]

Second: Revocation. This is more severe than suspension. You cannot drive at all until the MVA determines you can safely drive again. There is a minimum waiting period before you can even request reinstatement, and the MVA can deny your request.

[PAUSE]

Third: Cancellation. This is the most severe. Your driving privilege is permanently terminated and not reinstated. The MVA may cancel if you should not have been given a license, provided false information, or committed fraud.

[PAUSE]

Here is the memory trick: think S-R-C for suspension, revocation, cancellation. Each step is more severe. Suspension is temporary. Revocation requires a waiting period. Cancellation is permanent.

[PAUSE]

And an important detail: suspension and revocation periods begin when the MVA receives your license, not when the violation happened.

[PAUSE]

Now let us cover penalties for moving violations with a Provisional License.

[PAUSE]

For minors under 18: the first offense requires a driver improvement program. A second offense results in a 30-day suspension followed by a 90-day restriction to employment and education only. A third offense means a 180-day suspension, a driver improvement program for young drivers, and a 180-day restriction. A fourth or more offense results in license revocation, and you must pass all licensing tests again.

[PAUSE]

For adults 18 and older: the first offense requires a driver improvement program. A second offense results in a 30-day suspension. A third or more offense results in up to 180 days of suspension or revocation.

[PAUSE]

And here is what students always forget: these penalties are in addition to any court penalties. They do not replace them. Plus, after each conviction or probation before judgment, you must begin a new 18-month conviction-free period.

[PAUSE]

Let me say that again. The 18-month clock resets after every single violation. It does not carry over.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [11:30 - 13:30] Disability Parking and Quick Review

One more topic before we wrap up: disability parking.

[PAUSE]

You can receive big fines for illegally using disability parking spaces, plates, or placards. Here is what you need to know.

[PAUSE]

You can only park in a disability space if you have a disability license plate or placard issued by the MVA, and the person who qualified must be the driver or a passenger. You cannot use someone else's placard if they are not in the car.

[PAUSE]

You also cannot park in front of or block any part of a curb cut, even if you have a disability plate. And you cannot park on any part of an access aisle next to a disability space. An access aisle is the area people in wheelchairs use to get in and out of their vehicle.

[PAUSE]

Using an expired placard, using a placard without the qualifying person present, or any fraud when applying for a disability plate or placard are all citable offenses.

[PAUSE]

And one more thing: if you are caught drinking and driving, you will have to install a breathalyzer car lock that prevents you from starting the vehicle if alcohol is detected.

[PAUSE]

Let us do a quick review of the must-know facts.

[PAUSE]

Driving is a privilege. Four restrictions for provisional drivers under 18: seat belt, passenger for 151 days, nighttime from midnight to 5 a.m., and wireless device ban.

[PAUSE]

Implied consent means driving equals consent to testing. BAC of .08 triggers suspension. Refusing also triggers suspension.

[PAUSE]

Suspension is temporary. Revocation is more serious with a waiting period. Cancellation is permanent and not reinstated.

[PAUSE]

Minors: first offense is a program. Second is 30 days plus 90 days restricted. Third is 180 days plus 180 days restricted. Fourth is revoked.

[PAUSE]

Adults: first is a program. Second is 30 days. Third and beyond is up to 180 days.

[PAUSE]

The 18-month clock resets after each violation. Penalties are in addition to court penalties.

[PAUSE]

And disability parking requires the qualifying person to be present.
