## Questions

**Q1. [Type: RECALL]** What does "implied consent" mean under Maryland law?

**Answer:** By driving or attempting to drive on any Maryland road or public-use private property, you automatically consent to take a drug or alcohol test if a law enforcement officer requests it.

**Explanation:** Maryland's implied consent law means that the act of driving itself constitutes agreement to submit to testing. This applies to blood tests administered by a drug recognition expert when an officer has reason to believe you are impaired by drugs, alcohol, or a controlled substance.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often think implied consent only applies to alcohol tests — it covers drugs and controlled substances as well.

**Common Mistake:** Believing you can refuse a test without consequences — a certified refusal statement from an officer results in automatic license suspension.

---

**Q2. [Type: RECALL]** At what blood alcohol concentration (BAC) will the MVA suspend your license?

**Answer:** .08 percent or higher.

**Explanation:** Maryland law sets the legal BAC limit at .08 percent. If a test shows your BAC at or above this level, the MVA will suspend your license. This is separate from any court penalties for driving under the influence.

**MVA Test Trap:** Some students confuse .08 with .05 or .10 — the Maryland legal limit is specifically .08.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking BAC only applies to alcohol — drug impairment also results in license suspension.

---

**Q3. [Type: RECALL]** What is the passenger restriction for provisional drivers under 18?

**Answer:** For the first 151 days, you may not drive unsupervised with passengers under 18 unless they are immediate family members.

**Explanation:** The passenger restriction limits new drivers from carrying teen passengers, which is a leading risk factor for new driver crashes. The restriction applies specifically to the first 151 days of holding a Provisional License.

**MVA Test Trap:** The number is 151 days, not 180 or 150 — this specific number is a common test target.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the restriction applies only to friends, not relatives — it covers all non-family passengers under 18.

---

**Q4. [Type: RECALL]** What are the four exceptions that allow a provisional driver to drive between midnight and 5:00 a.m.?

**Answer:** Driving to, from, or during: (1) your job, (2) an organized volunteer program, (3) an official school activity, or (4) an athletic event or related training session you participate in.

**Explanation:** The nighttime restriction recognizes that teens need to drive for essential activities. Social outings, hanging out with friends, or personal errands do not qualify.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often think visiting friends or going to a party qualifies — it does not. Only the four specific exceptions apply.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the exception includes travel "during" the activity, not just to and from.

---

**Q5. [Type: RECALL]** What is the wireless device restriction for provisional license holders and learner's permit holders?

**Answer:** You may not use any wireless communication device while driving, including hands-free phones. The only exception is a 911 emergency call.

**Explanation:** Maryland imposes a strict wireless device ban on the newest, most vulnerable drivers. This includes texting, talking, and any other wireless communication — even hands-free devices.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students assume "hands-free is legal" — for provisional and permit holders, even hands-free is prohibited.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the restriction only applies to texting, not to all wireless communication.

---

**Q6. [Type: RECALL]** Who qualifies as "immediate family" under the passenger restriction?

**Answer:** Spouse, daughter, son, stepdaughter, stepson, sister, brother, stepsister, stepbrother, or any relative who lives at your address.

**Explanation:** The definition is broad and includes step-relatives and anyone residing at the same address. This allows provisional drivers to transport family members without violating the restriction.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often think only parents and siblings count — the definition includes step-relatives and household members.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that a relative living at your address qualifies even if not a direct family member.

---

**Q7. [Type: RECALL]** What happens if you try to obtain a fake identification card or driver's license in Maryland?

**Answer:** You may be fined and/or sent to prison under federal and state law, and your driver's license may be suspended.

**Explanation:** Obtaining fraudulent identification documents is a serious offense that carries both criminal penalties (fines and imprisonment) and administrative penalties (license suspension). Lying about your age to purchase alcohol carries the same consequences.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think only criminal penalties apply — license suspension is an additional administrative consequence.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking penalties only apply to creating fake IDs, not to lying about age for alcohol.

---

**Q8. [Type: RECALL]** What must you do if your license is suspended, revoked, refused, or cancelled?

**Answer:** You must surrender your license to the MVA. You may not drive until the action is resolved.

**Explanation:** The MVA requires physical surrender of your license. Suspension or revocation periods begin when the MVA receives your license, not when the action was imposed.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think you can keep your license and just not drive — you must physically surrender it.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the suspension/revocation period starts when the MVA receives the license, not when the violation occurred.

---

**Q9. [Type: RECALL]** What is the difference between license suspension and license revocation?

**Answer:** Suspension is a temporary loss of driving privilege; revocation is more severe — you cannot drive until the MVA determines you can safely drive again, and there is a minimum waiting period before you can request reinstatement.

**Explanation:** Suspension allows for a possible restricted license (for work/school), while revocation means no driving at all during the waiting period. The MVA can deny a reinstatement request even after the waiting period.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often confuse the two — suspension is temporary and may allow restricted driving, revocation is more serious with a mandatory waiting period.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking revocation has no waiting period or that you automatically get your license back after revocation.

---

**Q10. [Type: RECALL]** What does "cancellation" of a driver's license mean, and how does it differ from suspension or revocation?

**Answer:** Cancellation means your driving privilege is permanently terminated and NOT reinstated. It is more severe than both suspension and revocation.

**Explanation:** While suspension is temporary and revocation allows for potential reinstatement, cancellation permanently ends your driving privilege. The MVA may cancel if you should not have been given a license, provided false information, or committed fraud.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think cancellation is less severe than revocation — it is actually the MOST severe of the three actions.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming you can apply for reinstatement after cancellation — you cannot.

---

**Q11. [Type: APPLICATION]** A 17-year-old with a Provisional License drives her friend (age 16) to a movie at 10:00 p.m. She has held her license for 3 months. Is this legal?

**Answer:** No — this violates the passenger restriction. The 16-year-old friend is not immediate family, and it has been only about 90 days, well under the 151-day restriction period.

**Explanation:** The passenger restriction prohibits driving unsupervised with non-family passengers under 18 for the first 151 days. Since 3 months is approximately 90 days and the passenger is not family, this is a violation.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think nighttime rules are the issue — the passenger restriction is the actual violation here.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the passenger restriction timeline (151 days) with other timeframes.

---

**Q12. [Type: APPLICATION]** You are pulled over and the officer asks you to submit to a blood test. You believe you are sober and refuse. What happens?

**Answer:** The MVA will suspend your license based on the officer's certified refusal statement.

**Explanation:** Under implied consent law, refusing a test triggers automatic administrative suspension. Your actual guilt or innocence is irrelevant to the suspension — the refusal itself is the violation.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students think refusing avoids any consequences — refusal alone causes suspension.

**Common Mistake:** Believing that being sober gives you the right to refuse — it does not under implied consent.

---

**Q13. [Type: APPLICATION]** A provisional driver is convicted of a moving violation for the second time. What penalties apply if the driver is 16 years old?

**Answer:** 30-day license suspension followed by a 90-day restriction limiting driving to employment and educational purposes only.

**Explanation:** For minors (under 18) with a Provisional License, a second moving violation conviction or PBJ results in a 30-day suspension, then a 90-day employment/education-only restriction. This is in addition to any court-imposed penalties.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may apply adult penalties instead of minor penalties — the penalty structure differs by age.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that the 30-day suspension comes first, THEN the 90-day restriction.

---

**Q14. [Type: APPLICATION]** You have a disability placard but you're running into a store quickly. You park in a disability space even though the person with the disability stayed in the car at home. Can you be cited?

**Answer:** Yes — you can be cited because the person who qualified for the placard is not present in the vehicle.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires that the person who qualified for the disability placard or plate must be either the driver or a passenger. Using the placard without the qualifying person is a citable offense.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students think having the placard is enough — the qualifying person must actually be present.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming a placard can be used by anyone in the family — it belongs to the person who qualified.

---

**Q15. [Type: APPLICATION]** A driver's license is cancelled because the holder provided false information on the application. Can this person reapply for a license?

**Answer:** Cancellation means the driving privilege is terminated and not reinstated. The person would need to go through the full licensing process again, but the MVA may deny the application based on the fraud.

**Explanation:** Cancellation permanently ends the current license. While a new application is theoretically possible, the MVA's determination that fraud occurred would likely affect any future application.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think cancellation is temporary like suspension — it is permanent.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing cancellation (permanent) with suspension (temporary).

---

**Q16. [Type: APPLICATION]** You are 19 with a Provisional License and get your first moving violation conviction. Your court hearing is next month. What MVA penalty will you face?

**Answer:** You will be required to complete a driver improvement program.

**Explanation:** For adults (18+) with a Provisional License, the first moving violation conviction or PBJ triggers a mandatory driver improvement program. A second offense would result in a 30-day suspension.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think a first offense carries no penalty for adults — the driver improvement program is mandatory.

**Common Mistake:** Applying minor penalty structure (which includes longer restrictions) to adults.

---

**Q17. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Compare and contrast suspension, revocation, and cancellation of a Maryland driver's license in terms of severity, duration, and possibility of reinstatement.

**Answer:** Suspension is the least severe — it is temporary, may allow a restricted license for work/school, and you can regain full driving privileges after the suspension period. Revocation is more severe — there is a minimum waiting period before you can request reinstatement, and the MVA can deny your request. Cancellation is the most severe — your driving privilege is permanently terminated and cannot be reinstated. The MVA may cancel if you obtained the license through fraud, provided false information, or should not have been given a license.

**Explanation:** These three actions form a hierarchy of severity. The key distinction is that suspension is temporary with possible restricted driving, revocation requires a waiting period and MVA approval for reinstatement, and cancellation is permanent with no reinstatement possible.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often rank revocation as the most severe — cancellation is actually the most severe because it is permanent and not reinstated.

**Common Mistake:** Not recognizing that all three require license surrender and that the period begins when the MVA receives your license.

---

**Q18. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Explain the difference in moving violation penalties between a 16-year-old and a 19-year-old, both with Provisional Licenses, after their second offense.

**Answer:** The 16-year-old faces a 30-day suspension followed by a 90-day employment/education-only restriction. The 19-year-old faces only a 30-day suspension. The minor receives the additional restriction period because Maryland's graduated licensing system imposes stricter penalties on younger drivers.

**Explanation:** Maryland's penalty structure is age-dependent. Minors (under 18) face escalating restrictions including employment/education-only limits after suspension. Adults (18+) with Provisional Licenses face simpler penalties — suspension without the additional restriction period.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think penalties are the same regardless of age — the minor penalty structure includes additional restrictions.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that both penalties are IN ADDITION to any court-imposed penalties.

---

**Q19. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** How do Maryland's implied consent laws interact with the administrative penalty system? Explain what happens when someone both refuses a test AND is later convicted in court.

**Answer:** Refusing a test triggers immediate administrative suspension through the MVA, based on the officer's certified refusal statement. This happens regardless of court outcomes. If you are later convicted in court, you face additional court penalties (fines, possible jail, etc.) AND the MVA imposes its own penalties (suspension, possible revocation). The administrative action and court penalties are independent — one does not replace the other. This is why Maryland law states that MVA penalties are imposed "in addition to" court penalties.

**Explanation:** The two systems — administrative (MVA) and judicial (court) — operate independently. You can face penalties from both. A refusal alone causes MVA suspension; a conviction causes court penalties plus additional MVA penalties.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think court penalties replace MVA penalties — they are cumulative.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming that a not-guilty court verdict reverses the administrative suspension — it does not automatically.

---

**Q20. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Explain how the wireless device restriction for provisional drivers relates to the broader graduated driver licensing philosophy. Why might Maryland apply stricter rules to new drivers?

**Answer:** The wireless device restriction — which bans ALL wireless communication including hands-free for provisional and permit holders — reflects the graduated licensing philosophy that new drivers need fewer distractions while they develop driving skills. The MVA applies stricter rules to new drivers because: (1) novice drivers are statistically more crash-prone, (2) they have less experience managing multiple tasks while driving, and (3) research shows that any phone use (even hands-free) significantly impairs a new driver's attention. The wireless device ban is part of a broader set of restrictions — along with passenger, nighttime, and seat belt restrictions — designed to reduce risk during the most vulnerable period of driving.

**Explanation:** Graduated licensing systematically reduces risk factors for new drivers. The wireless device restriction is one of four key restrictions that together address the main causes of teen crashes: distraction (wireless device), peer pressure (passenger), fatigue/poor visibility (nighttime), and occupant protection (seat belt).

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may think the wireless device restriction is arbitrary — it is based on crash data and distraction research.

**Common Mistake:** Not connecting the wireless device restriction to the other three provisional restrictions as part of a unified safety philosophy.
