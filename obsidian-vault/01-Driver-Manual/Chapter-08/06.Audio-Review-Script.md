## [1:00 - 4:00] Crash Response: Injury Scenarios

Let's start with what to do after a crash. The rules depend on whether anyone is injured and whether your vehicle can move.

[PAUSE]

First, if you are involved in a crash where someone has been injured, including a pedestrian or bicyclist, you must remain at the scene and do three things. Call nine-one-one immediately. Report the number of people involved, the type of injury, and the location of the crash. And do not move the vehicles.

[PAUSE]

If you are convicted of leaving the scene of a crash that results in a serious injury, you will face severe penalties. Your license will be revoked. You could go to prison for up to five years. And you could be fined up to five thousand dollars.

[PAUSE]

If someone dies, it gets worse. Leaving the scene of a crash where someone dies is a felony. You could go to prison for up to ten years and be fined up to ten thousand dollars.

[PAUSE]

So remember. Injury equals five years and five thousand dollars. Death equals ten years and ten thousand dollars. Both are devastating consequences.

[PAUSE]

If there are no injuries but your vehicle cannot move, call nine-one-one immediately. Give the location of the crash. Report that there are no injuries but you need police assistance. Be patient and do not try to cross the road or stop traffic. Make sure you stay away from traffic.

[PAUSE]

If there are no injuries and your vehicle can move, move your vehicle off the road as close as possible to where the crash occurred. This will help keep you and others safe and prevent traffic backups.

[PAUSE]

Then get important information from the other driver or drivers. Give them the same information. This includes name, address, phone number, license plate number and state, driver's license number, vehicle make and model, and insurance information.

[PAUSE]

Ask witnesses for their names, addresses, and phone numbers. Remember the location, date and time, number of vehicles involved, weather conditions, and road conditions. Write this down if you can. Remember any damages to the vehicles. Take pictures if you can. Write down the facts of how the crash happened.

[PAUSE]

You must always call the police when someone has been injured, when a vehicle cannot be moved, when a driver appears to be under the influence of alcohol or drugs, when a driver does not have a license, when a driver tries to leave without giving information, or when public property has been damaged.

[PAUSE]

If you strike an unattended vehicle or property, you are required to stop, try to find the owner, and if you cannot find them, leave a note with your contact information where they can easily find it.

[PAUSE]

And if you hit and injure a domestic animal, you must immediately call the police. Dogs, cats, horses, and cows are examples of domestic animals.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [4:00 - 7:30] Roadside Emergencies and Traffic Stops

Now let's cover roadside emergencies and traffic stops.

[PAUSE]

Every roadside emergency is different. Assess the situation, use good judgment, and watch for other vehicles because emergencies can change in an instant.

[PAUSE]

Find a safe place. Every year, people are injured or killed on the side of the road while waiting for assistance. If your vehicle becomes disabled, do your best to move it to the shoulder. If you can reach an off-ramp or parking lot, consider doing that even if it means driving on a flat tire or damaging a rim. The further you can be from traffic, the safer you will be.

[PAUSE]

Never stop or park in the triangular shaped painted zones where a ramp exits the road.

[PAUSE]

If you can remain in your vehicle, stay buckled and face forward. Vehicles have safety features that will help protect you and your passengers if another vehicle hits your vehicle.

[PAUSE]

If you cannot remain in your vehicle, watch what is happening around you and pick the safest time to get out. Do not stand near the vehicle. Instead, move quickly away from the road. Get behind an embankment, traffic barrier, or guardrail. If possible, move to higher ground. Face oncoming traffic and remain alert.

[PAUSE]

Now, traffic stops. Law enforcement officers issue written traffic citations to people charged with violating the Maryland Motor Vehicle Law. You must follow the officer's directions or you may be arrested.

[PAUSE]

If a law enforcement officer stops you, here is the proper procedure. Pull off to the side of the road as far away from traffic as possible. Turn on your flashers. Turn off your engine and radio. Roll down your window so you can communicate with the officer. Stay in your vehicle and keep your seat belt fastened. Keep your hands in plain view on the steering wheel is best. Do not make any movement that will make the officer think you are hiding or reaching for something.

[PAUSE]

If the officer issues you a citation, do not argue with the officer about the citation. You will have your chance to make your case if you go to court.

[PAUSE]

Think of the traffic stop as six steps. Pull over, flashers on, engine off, window down, hands visible, and stay calm.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [7:30 - 9:30] The Move Over Law

Now let's cover Maryland's Move Over Law. This is a very commonly tested topic.

[PAUSE]

In Maryland, you must move over when approaching any vehicles that are stopped, standing, or parked on a road with their lights flashing. If it is not safe or possible to move over, you must slow down to a safe speed. Consider the weather, road conditions, traffic, and pedestrians when deciding a safe speed.

[PAUSE]

The penalties are tiered. Violating this law is a misdemeanor. The base penalty is a one hundred ten dollar fine and one point on your license.

[PAUSE]

If the violation causes a crash, the penalty increases to a one hundred fifty dollar fine and three points.

[PAUSE]

If there is a death or serious injury, the penalty is a seven hundred fifty dollar fine and three points.

[PAUSE]

Points lead to your license being suspended or revoked. The MVA uses points to track violations and issue penalties.

[PAUSE]

So remember the three tiers. Base: one hundred ten dollars and one point. Crash: one hundred fifty dollars and three points. Death or serious injury: seven hundred fifty dollars and three points.

[PAUSE]

The Move Over Law applies to all stopped vehicles with flashing lights, including emergency vehicles, tow trucks, and service vehicles.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [9:30 - 11:30] Quick Review

Let's do a rapid recap of must-know facts from Chapter Eight.

[PAUSE]

Crash with injury: stay, call nine-one-one, report details, do not move vehicles.

[PAUSE]

Hit-and-run with injury: license revoked, up to five years prison, five thousand dollar fine. Hit-and-run with death: felony, up to ten years prison, ten thousand dollar fine.

[PAUSE]

No injury, can't move: call nine-one-one, stay away from traffic. No injury, can move: move off road, exchange information, get witnesses, document everything.

[PAUSE]

Call police when: someone injured, vehicle immobilized, driver impaired, no license, attempted flee, public property damaged.

[PAUSE]

Unattended property: stop, find owner, leave note with contact info. Domestic animal hit: call police immediately.

[PAUSE]

Roadside emergency: stay buckled if safe; if exit needed, move behind barrier, face traffic, stay alert.

[PAUSE]

Traffic stop: pull far off, flashers, engine off, window down, hands on wheel, stay belted. Do not argue about citations.

[PAUSE]

Move Over Law: move over or slow for flashing lights. Base penalty: one hundred ten dollars and one point. Crash: one hundred fifty dollars and three points. Death or serious injury: seven hundred fifty dollars and three points.

[PAUSE]

That covers Chapter Eight. Replay this for spaced repetition. The MVA test rewards you for knowing these facts cold.
