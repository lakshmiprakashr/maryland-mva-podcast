# Chapter-08 - Rapid Recall Drill

## Instructions
Answer all 20 questions. Check answers after completing. Target time: 25-30 minutes.

## Questions

**Q1. [Type: RECALL]** What must you do immediately after being involved in a crash where someone has been injured?

**Answer:** Remain at the scene, call 911 immediately, report the number of people involved, the type of injury, and the location of the crash. Do not move the vehicles.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires you to stay at the scene and call for help. Moving vehicles can compromise evidence and endanger injured persons.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you can move vehicles to clear traffic — you must not move them when someone is injured.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting to report the number of people and type of injury to 911.

---

**Q2. [Type: RECALL]** What are the penalties for leaving the scene of a crash that results in serious injury?

**Answer:** Your license will be revoked. You could go to prison for up to 5 years and be fined up to $5,000.

**Explanation:** Leaving the scene of a crash with serious injuries is a severe offense in Maryland. All three penalties — revocation, imprisonment, and fines — apply.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing the penalties with the death scenario — injury is $5,000 and 5 years; death is $10,000 and 10 years.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking only the fine applies — license revocation and prison time are also consequences.

---

**Q3. [Type: RECALL]** What are the penalties for leaving the scene of a crash where someone dies?

**Answer:** It is a felony. You could go to prison for up to 10 years and be fined up to $10,000.

**Explanation:** Hit-and-run resulting in death is treated as a felony in Maryland with the most severe penalties in this chapter.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing with the injury penalties — death doubles both the prison time and the fine.

**Common Mistake:** Not recognizing that this is a felony offense, not just a misdemeanor.

---

**Q4. [Type: RECALL]** What information must you exchange with other drivers after a no-injury crash where vehicles can move?

**Answer:** Name, address, phone number, license plate number and state, driver's license number, vehicle make and model, and insurance information.

**Explanation:** This is the complete list of information that must be exchanged. Both parties give and receive the same information.

**MVA Test Trap:** Forgetting any item from the list — the MVA test may ask specifically about insurance or license plate details.

**Common Mistake:** Not getting the other driver's insurance information.

---

**Q5. [Type: RECALL]** When must you always call the police after a crash?

**Answer:** When someone has been injured, when a vehicle cannot be moved, when a driver appears to be under the influence of alcohol or drugs, when a driver does not have a license, when a driver tries to leave without providing information, or when public property has been damaged.

**Explanation:** These six situations always require police involvement, regardless of crash severity.

**MVA Test Trap:** Forgetting that impaired driving or unlicensed driving requires police even if there are no injuries.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking police are only needed when someone is injured.

---

**Q6. [Type: RECALL]** What must you do if you strike an unattended vehicle or unattended property?

**Answer:** Stop your vehicle as close as possible to where the crash occurred without blocking traffic. Try to find the owner. If you cannot find the owner, leave a note with what happened and your contact information where they can easily find it.

**Explanation:** The law requires you to make a reasonable effort to locate the owner and provide your information, even if they are not present.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you can just drive away if no one is around — you must stop and leave a note.

**Common Mistake:** Not leaving a note or leaving it in a place where the owner won't find it.

---

**Q7. [Type: RECALL]** What must you do if you hit and injure a domestic animal with your vehicle?

**Answer:** You must immediately call the police. Dogs, cats, horses, and cows are examples of domestic animals.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires immediate police notification for domestic animal collisions. This is a frequently tested fact.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you can just leave or handle it yourself — calling police is mandatory.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing that domestic animals include dogs, cats, horses, and cows.

---

**Q8. [Type: RECALL]** What is the base penalty for violating Maryland's Move Over Law?

**Answer:** A $110 fine and one point on your license.

**Explanation:** The Move Over Law requires you to move over or slow down for stopped vehicles with flashing lights. The base penalty is the lowest tier.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing with the enhanced penalties — $110/1 point is the base; $150/3 points for crash; $750/3 points for death.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the penalty is the same regardless of consequences.

---

**Q9. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're driving and see a disabled vehicle on the shoulder ahead. Traffic is moving at 55 mph. What should you do?

**Answer:** Move to a lane not immediately adjacent to the stopped vehicle. If that's not possible, slow down to a safe speed and prepare to stop if necessary.

**Explanation:** This is the Move Over Law. It applies to all stopped vehicles with flashing lights, not just emergency vehicles.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the Move Over Law only applies to police cars — it covers all stopped vehicles with flashing lights.

**Common Mistake:** Maintaining speed in the lane next to a disabled vehicle.

---

**Q10. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're in a minor fender-bender with no injuries and both cars can drive. What should you do first?

**Answer:** Move your vehicle off the road as close as possible to where the crash occurred, then exchange information with the other driver.

**Explanation:** Moving vehicles off the road prevents traffic backups and keeps everyone safer. Then exchange information and document the scene.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you should stay in the road and wait for police — only required if someone is injured or vehicles can't move.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting to get witness information and take pictures.

---

**Q11. [Type: APPLICATION]** A police officer turns on their lights behind you on a busy highway. What should you do?

**Answer:** Pull off to the side of the road as far away from traffic as possible. Turn on your flashers. Turn off your engine and radio. Roll down your window. Stay in your vehicle with your seat belt fastened. Keep your hands on the steering wheel.

**Explanation:** This is the proper traffic stop procedure. Every step is designed to keep both you and the officer safe.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you can stay in the travel lane — you must pull as far off the road as possible.

**Common Mistake:** Reaching for your glove compartment or pockets before the officer approaches.

---

**Q12. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're pulled over and the officer hands you a citation. What should you do?

**Answer:** Do not argue with the officer about the citation. You will have your chance to make your case if you go to court.

**Explanation:** Arguing with the officer at the roadside is never productive and could escalate the situation. The court is the proper place to contest a citation.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you should explain your side at the roadside — the manual explicitly says not to argue.

**Common Mistake:** Becoming confrontational instead of accepting the citation politely.

---

**Q13. [Type: APPLICATION]** Your car breaks down on the highway shoulder and you cannot move it. You're alone and it's dark. What should you do?

**Answer:** Stay buckled and face forward in your vehicle if it's safe. If it's not safe, exit when traffic is clear, move quickly away from the road, get behind a barrier or guardrail, face oncoming traffic, and remain alert.

**Explanation:** The manual gives two paths: stay in the vehicle if safe, or move to higher ground behind a barrier if not. Staying belted protects you if another vehicle hits yours.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you should always exit the vehicle — staying inside is safer when the vehicle is in a protected position.

**Common Mistake:** Standing near the vehicle on the shoulder instead of moving to safety.

---

**Q14. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're approaching a tow truck stopped on the highway shoulder with its amber lights flashing. What should you do?

**Answer:** Move to a lane not immediately adjacent to the tow truck. If that's not possible, slow down to a safe speed and prepare to stop.

**Explanation:** The Move Over Law applies to tow trucks and service vehicles, not just police cars and ambulances.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the Move Over Law only applies to emergency vehicles with red/blue lights — amber lights on tow trucks count too.

**Common Mistake:** Maintaining speed in the lane next to the tow truck.

---

**Q15. [Type: APPLICATION]** You back into a parked car in a parking lot and no one is around. What should you do?

**Answer:** Stop your vehicle as close as possible to where the crash occurred. Try to find the owner. If you cannot find the owner, leave a note with what happened and your contact information where they can easily find it.

**Explanation:** This is the unattended vehicle/property law. Even in a parking lot, you must make a reasonable effort to locate the owner and provide your information.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you can drive away if it's "just a parking lot" — the law applies everywhere.

**Common Mistake:** Not leaving a note because you think it's minor damage.

---

**Q16. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Compare the three tiers of Move Over Law penalties. What factors determine which tier applies?

**Answer:** Tier 1: $110 fine and one point — base violation. Tier 2: $150 fine and three points — if the violation causes a crash. Tier 3: $750 fine and three points — if there is a death or serious injury. The escalating factors are whether a crash results and whether death or serious injury occurs.

**Explanation:** Maryland uses a tiered penalty system that reflects the severity of consequences. The base penalty is relatively mild, but the consequences escalate dramatically if the violation causes harm.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing the point values — the base is 1 point, but both enhanced tiers are 3 points.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the penalty is always the same regardless of outcome.

---

**Q17. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** What are the key differences between crash scenarios requiring different responses? Compare injury crashes, no-injury with immobilized vehicles, and no-injury with mobile vehicles.

**Answer:** Injury crash: remain at scene, call 911, report details, do not move vehicles. No injury but vehicle can't move: call 911 for police assistance, stay away from traffic. No injury and vehicle can move: move off road, exchange information, get witnesses, document everything. The key differentiator is whether anyone is hurt and whether vehicles can be moved.

**Explanation:** Each scenario has a specific protocol. The MVA test frequently asks you to identify which protocol applies based on the situation described.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you always need to call 911 after any crash — only certain situations require it.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the protocols for immobilized versus mobile vehicles.

---

**Q18. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** How does the hit-and-run penalty structure reflect Maryland's priorities? Compare injury versus death penalties.

**Answer:** Injury hit-and-run: license revoked, up to 5 years prison, $5,000 fine. Death hit-and-run: felony, up to 10 years prison, $10,000 fine. Both prison time and fines double from injury to death. The felony classification for death reflects Maryland's priority on accountability when lives are lost. The license revocation ensures dangerous drivers cannot continue driving.

**Explanation:** The escalating penalties show Maryland treats hit-and-run as increasingly serious when consequences are more severe. The doubled penalties for death versus injury send a clear message.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the penalties are the same for injury and death — they double for death.

**Common Mistake:** Forgetting that license revocation applies to both scenarios.

---

**Q19. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** What are the common elements across all traffic stop procedures, and why does each step matter?

**Answer:** Common elements: pull far off road (safety from traffic), flashers on (visibility), engine off (safety), window down (communication), stay in vehicle (officer safety), seat belt on (your safety), hands visible on steering wheel (officer safety). Each step serves a specific purpose: protecting you from traffic, making you visible, preventing vehicle movement, enabling communication, and ensuring the officer feels safe approaching.

**Explanation:** The traffic stop procedure is designed with both your safety and the officer's safety in mind. Every step has a practical reason behind it.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking some steps are optional — the manual presents them as a complete protocol.

**Common Mistake:** Skipping steps like turning off the engine or keeping hands visible.

---

**Q20. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** How do the roadside emergency rules for staying in your vehicle versus exiting connect to the overall theme of crash prevention and safety?

**Answer:** If you can remain in your vehicle, stay buckled and face forward — vehicles have safety features that protect you. If you cannot remain, exit to higher ground behind a barrier, face traffic, and stay alert. This connects to crash prevention because being aware of your surroundings and choosing the safest option — whether inside or outside the vehicle — reduces your risk of being hit by passing traffic. The manual emphasizes that roadside emergencies can change in an instant, so constant vigilance is key.

**Explanation:** The decision to stay or exit is based on a risk assessment. The vehicle offers built-in protection, but if positioned dangerously, moving to safety behind a barrier is better. Both choices require awareness and quick judgment.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking there's one always-correct answer — the manual presents two paths based on safety assessment.

**Common Mistake:** Not considering whether the vehicle is in a dangerous position when deciding to stay or exit.

## Quick Reference

- Injury crash: stay, call 911, report details, don't move vehicles
- Hit-and-run injury: license revoked, up to 5 years, $5,000
- Hit-and-run death: felony, up to 10 years, $10,000
- No injury, can't move: call 911, stay away from traffic
- No injury, can move: move off road, exchange info, document everything
- Call police when: injury, immobilized vehicle, impaired driver, no license, attempted flee, public property damage
- Unattended property: stop, find owner, leave note
- Domestic animal hit: call police immediately
- Traffic stop: pull far off, flashers, engine off, window down, hands on wheel, stay belted
- Move Over Law: $110/1 point base; $150/3 points crash; $750/3 points death/injury
- Points lead to license suspension or revocation

**Test Day Tip:** When you see a crash scenario question, first determine: Is anyone injured? Can the vehicles move? Your answer to these two questions determines which protocol applies.
