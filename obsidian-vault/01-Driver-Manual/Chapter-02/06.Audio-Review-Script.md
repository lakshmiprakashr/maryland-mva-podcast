## [1:30] Section 1: What You Need to Apply

Before we talk about the licensing stages, let's cover what you need to bring to the MVA when you apply.

[PAUSE]

To get a Maryland driver's license, you must visit any MVA branch with an appointment. You will need four things.

[PAUSE]

First, a document that proves your age and includes your full name. Think birth certificate or passport.

[PAUSE]

Second, if you were born outside the United States, you need a document that proves your lawful status.

[PAUSE]

Third, your Social Security Number, or proof that you are not eligible for one.

[PAUSE]

Fourth, and this is the one people forget, you need two documents that prove you live in Maryland. Not one. Two.

[PAUSE]

Now here is a critical rule. The MVA will only accept original documents or copies that are certified by the agency that issued them. No photocopies. No notarized copies. And absolutely no documents with any changes or erased information. If your document looks like someone tried to alter it, the MVA will reject it.

[PAUSE]

The MVA also issues a special noncommercial driver's license called a Class C license. This covers regular vehicles like cars, pickup trucks, and vans. That is the license most people are applying for.

[PAUSE]

Let me repeat the key takeaway here. You need proof of age, proof of lawful status if applicable, your Social Security Number, and two Maryland residency documents. Originals or certified copies only.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [4:00] Section 2: The Graduated Driver Licensing System

Now let's get into the heart of this chapter, the Rookie Driver Graduated Driver Licensing System. The acronym is G-D-L.

[PAUSE]

The G-D-L system is the process you go through to get a Maryland driver's license if you have never had one before. And here is the important thing to remember. It applies to people of all ages. Not just teenagers. If you are forty years old and have never held a license, you still go through the G-D-L.

[PAUSE]

The system has three stages. Stage one is the Learner's Instructional Permit. Stage two is the Provisional License. And stage three is the full Driver's License.

[PAUSE]

Let us start with stage one, the Learner's Instructional Permit.

[PAUSE]

There are two types of permits. Type one and Type two.

[PAUSE]

Type one is for first-time drivers who have never held a license. You must pass a vision screening and a knowledge test to get it. A Type one permit is valid for up to two years.

[PAUSE]

Type two is different. It is for people who already have a license but want a different class. You must be at least sixteen years and six months old. A Type two permit lasts up to one hundred eighty days. And you can take your driving skills test after holding it for just fourteen days.

[PAUSE]

Remember the difference. Type one equals first-timer, two years. Type two equals license class change, one hundred eighty days, sixteen and a half years old minimum.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [7:00] Section 3: Practice Hours and Hold Times

This is where the numbers get important. The MVA will definitely test you on these.

[PAUSE]

If you are under twenty-five, you must complete sixty practice hours with a supervising driver. Of those sixty hours, at least ten must be at night. Night means the period from thirty minutes before sunset through thirty minutes after sunrise.

[PAUSE]

If you are twenty-five or older, the requirement drops significantly. You need fourteen practice hours total, with at least three hours at night.

[PAUSE]

Let me say that again because the numbers are easy to mix up. Under twenty-five equals sixty hours total, ten at night. Twenty-five and older equals fourteen hours total, three at night.

[PAUSE]

Everyone must keep a Practice and Skills Log to record their hours. You can find this log on the MVA website.

[PAUSE]

Now let us talk about how long you must hold your permit before you can get a Provisional License. The baseline minimum is forty-five days for all applicants.

[PAUSE]

But here is where it gets more complicated. If you are under eighteen, you must hold the permit for at least nine months without any violations. If you are eighteen with a high school diploma or GED, or if you are between nineteen and twenty-four, you must hold it for at least three months without violations.

[PAUSE]

And here is the big one. At any age, if you are convicted of a moving violation or receive a **probation before judgment**, which is pronounced pro-BAY-shun be-FORE JUDGE-ment, you must hold the permit for at least nine months from the date of that conviction or probation. This also applies if you have been suspended for any reason.

[PAUSE]

So remember. Nine months for under eighteen with a clean record. Three months for eighteen to twenty-four with a diploma or GED. And nine months from any moving violation or probation before judgment, regardless of age.

[PAUSE]

One more thing. If you are under sixteen, you must provide a completed Learner's Permit School Attendance Certification form. That form is called the D-L-three-hundred.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [10:00] Section 4: Provisional License and Full License

Let us move to stage two of the G-D-L, the Provisional License.

[PAUSE]

A Provisional License lets you drive without a supervising driver, but with restrictions. Those restrictions include limitations on passengers and night driving.

[PAUSE]

To get a Provisional License, you must complete your driver education program, have a valid unexpired permit, hold the permit for the required time without violations, submit your completed Practice and Skills Log showing the required practice hours, and pass the driving skills test.

[PAUSE]

You will hold a Provisional License for at least eighteen months. After that, if you have not been convicted of any moving violations or received any probation before judgment during those eighteen months, the MVA will automatically switch your Provisional License to a full Driver's License.

[PAUSE]

Let me emphasize that. The switch is **automatic**. You do not need to go back to the MVA. They will mail you a card showing the switch, and you must carry that card along with your Provisional License.

[PAUSE]

To qualify for a full license, you must be at least eighteen years old, have held your Provisional for at least eighteen months, and have no moving violations or probation before judgment within those eighteen months.

[PAUSE]

The key number here is eighteen. Eighteen months of Provisional driving. Eighteen months of clean record. And age eighteen or older.

[LONG PAUSE]

## [12:30] Section 5: Co-Signers, Out-of-Country, and Special Cases

Before we wrap up, let us cover a few important special situations.

[PAUSE]

If you are under eighteen, you are considered a minor. Minors must have a parent or legal guardian co-sign their license application. You need to provide proof, like an original or certified birth certificate showing your parent's name, or court documents showing legal guardianship.

[PAUSE]

If you do not have a parent or guardian available, a responsible adult can co-sign. Your employer, for example. But you will need to provide documentation, like your parents' death certificates or proof of emancipation. Emancipation means a court has decided a minor is no longer under their parents' control.

[PAUSE]

A co-signer can cancel a minor's license at any time by sending a written request to the MVA's Administrative Adjudication Division. The license stays cancelled until the minor turns eighteen, unless a new co-signer signs a new application.

[PAUSE]

Now, if you have a license from another country, you face additional requirements. You must pass a vision screening, a knowledge test, a driving skills test, AND complete a three-hour MVA-approved roadway safety education program. That three-hour program is required for all out-of-country license holders.

[PAUSE]

If your license is not in English, you must also have an International License or get it translated. The translation must come from an MVA-approved provider, a court translator, or your country's embassy. And it must include the translator's printed name, phone number, and signature.

[PAUSE]

One more important thing. The MVA provides accommodations for customers with disabilities. This includes extra time on tests, sign language support, oral testing, screen readers, headphones, and visual aids. Contact feedback dot mva dot maryland dot gov for details.

[PAUSE]

And if you have a developmental disability, you can choose to carry a Developmental Disability Disclosure Card. It is a voluntary blue card the size of a driver's license. You can get one at any MVA location, and the MVA will not ask for documentation about your disability.

[PAUSE]

For foreign language speakers, the MVA offers tests in several languages. If the knowledge test is available in your language, you must attempt it five times before you can use an interpreter. The MVA does not charge for interpreter services, but you pay the interpreter directly.

[LONG PAUSE]
