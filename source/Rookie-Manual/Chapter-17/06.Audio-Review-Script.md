# Chapter 17 - Audio Review Script

## [0:00] Introduction

Welcome to your audio review for Maryland Driver's Manual chapter seventeen, covering **adverse weather conditions** and **parking skills**. [PAUSE]

This chapter is packed with information that shows up constantly on the Maryland knowledge test. We will walk through rain, fog, snow, hydroplaning, and then cover both parallel and angle parking step by step. [PAUSE]

Maryland weather can change quickly, and knowing how to respond to each condition could save your life. Let us get started. [LONG PAUSE]

## [1:30] Section 1: Rain and Wet Roads

When it rains, even lightly, there are two things you must do immediately. Turn on your **headlights** and turn on your **defoggers**. [PAUSE]

Headlights help you see, but more importantly, they help other drivers see you. Rain reduces visibility for everyone on the road. [PAUSE]

Your front and rear defoggers keep your windows clear. Wet weather puts moisture in the air and your windows will fog up quickly. [PAUSE]

Now here is a critical fact. It takes **longer to stop** on wet roadways. So you need to increase your following distance and drive slower than you normally would. [PAUSE]

When you brake on wet roads, brake **earlier** and **less forcefully**. Hard braking on wet pavement can cause your wheels to lock up and skid. Gentle, early braking keeps you in control and signals the driver behind you that you are slowing down. [PAUSE]

Watch for pedestrians in the rain. People get distracted opening umbrellas or rushing to get out of the downpour. They may step into the road without looking. [PAUSE]

And here is a rule the MVA test loves to ask about. If you cannot see the road or the car in front of you, or if you are feeling anxious, **pull over** to a safe spot. Do not try to push through zero visibility. [LONG PAUSE]

## [4:00] Section 2: Hydroplaning and Fog

Let us talk about **hydroplaning**. This is one of the most tested topics on the Maryland exam. [PAUSE]

Hydroplaning happens when water on the road is deeper than your tire tread and you are driving too fast. The water gets between your tires and the road, and you lose all friction. That means you lose steering and braking. [PAUSE]

If you start to hydroplane, **ease off the gas pedal**. Do not brake. Do not turn the wheel sharply. Just lift off the accelerator and let the car slow down until the tires reconnect with the road. [EMPHASIS: ease off] [PAUSE]

To avoid hydroplaning altogether, avoid deep puddles when possible. If you cannot avoid a puddle, slow down before you hit it. [PAUSE]

Now for fog. The rule here is simple and the MVA exam will test you on it. [PAUSE]

In fog, use **low beams only**. Here is why. High beams angle upward. When that light hits fog droplets, it bounces right back into your eyes. You end up with a wall of white glare and even less visibility than before. [PAUSE]

Low beams stay low. They cut through the fog without reflecting back. And if your vehicle has fog lights, use them too. They are specifically designed for these conditions. [LONG PAUSE]

## [7:00] Section 3: Snow and Ice

Snow and ice are where driving gets really serious, especially here in Maryland where conditions vary widely from the eastern shore to western mountains. [PAUSE]

Before you even start the car, make sure your **entire vehicle** is free of snow and ice. That means the roof, the hood, the trunk, all windows, and all mirrors. Snow flying off your car at highway speed can hit the windshield of the car behind you. [PAUSE]

When you are driving on snow, remember three words. **Smooth**, **slow**, and **gentle**. [EMPHASIS: smooth, slow, gentle] Steer smoothly, travel slowly, and brake gently. [PAUSE]

Here is the fact you must memorize. Stopping distances on snow are **ten times greater** than on dry roads. [EMPHASIS: ten times] That means if you normally need one hundred feet to stop, on snow you need one thousand feet. [PAUSE]

Watch for icy conditions, especially on **bridges**, **overpasses**, and in **shaded areas**. These spots freeze before the rest of the road because they lose heat faster. Bridges are exposed to cold air on both the top and bottom. [PAUSE]

The MVA exam will ask where ice forms first. The answer is always bridges, overpasses, and shaded areas. [LONG PAUSE]

## [10:00] Section 4: Parking Skills

Now let us shift to parking, which is tested on the MVA Skills Test. [PAUSE]

**Parallel parking** has a specific sequence. First, signal your intentions. Stop parallel to the vehicle you are parking behind with your front seat aligned with theirs. Leave **two to three feet** between your vehicles. [PAUSE]

With your foot on the brake, shift to reverse. Check traffic in all directions and yield to pedestrians, bicyclists, and other vehicles. [PAUSE]

Back slowly and turn the wheel sharply in the appropriate direction. Continue backing until you are in line with the rear bumper of the car behind you. Then turn the wheel the opposite direction and stop before touching that bumper. [PAUSE]

Shift to drive and adjust your position. Your vehicle must end up no more than **twelve inches** from the curb. That is the key number to remember. [EMPHASIS: twelve inches] [PAUSE]

For **angle parking**, signal your intentions, move slowly into the space, and center your vehicle. When exiting, check traffic in all directions, back up carefully, and turn the wheel toward the direction you want to travel. [LONG PAUSE]

## [12:30] Quick Review

Let us rapid-fire the must-know facts. [PAUSE]

Fog means low beams. Always. [PAUSE]

Rain means headlights on and brake early and gently. [PAUSE]

Hydroplaning response: ease off the gas, do not brake. [PAUSE]

Snow stopping distance: ten times greater than dry roads. [PAUSE]

Ice forms first on bridges, overpasses, and shaded areas. [PAUSE]

Parallel parking: twelve inches maximum from the curb. [PAUSE]

Parallel parking setup: two to three feet between vehicles. [PAUSE]

Always clear your entire vehicle of snow and ice before driving. [LONG PAUSE]

You have now reviewed two critical topics for the Maryland knowledge test and for real-world driving safety. Adverse weather is where experienced drivers separate from reckless ones, and parking is a skill that tests your spatial awareness and vehicle control. [PAUSE]
