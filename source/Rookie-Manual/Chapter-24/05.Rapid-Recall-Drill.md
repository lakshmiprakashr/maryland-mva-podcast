## Questions

**Q1. [Type: RECALL]** What is the minimum age for a Learner's Permit in Maryland?

**Answer:** 15 years and 9 months.

**Explanation:** The Learner's Permit is the first stage of Maryland's Graduated Licensing System, available at age 15 years and 9 months.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't confuse this with the provisional license age (16 years, 6 months) or full license age (18 years).

**Common Mistake:** Rounding to 15 or 16 instead of the exact 15 years, 9 months.

---

**Q2. [Type: RECALL]** What is the minimum age for a Provisional License?

**Answer:** 16 years and 6 months.

**Explanation:** The Provisional License is the second stage, available at 16 years and 6 months. It comes with specific restrictions for drivers under 18.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't confuse this with the Learner's Permit age or full license age.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming provisional license is available at exactly 16 years old.

---

**Q3. [Type: RECALL]** What is the minimum age for a full Driver License?

**Answer:** 18 years.

**Explanation:** A full, unrestricted Driver License is available at age 18, the final stage of the Graduated Licensing System.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't confuse this with the provisional license age.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the full license is available at 17 or earlier.

---

**Q4. [Type: RECALL]** Is a cosigner required for all Learner's Permit holders?

**Answer:** Yes — all Learner's Permit holders must have a cosigner who can request MVA cancel the permit for any reason.

**Explanation:** The cosigner requirement is universal for Learner's Permits. The cosigner has the authority to request cancellation of the permit at any time.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't think the cosigner requirement varies for Learner's Permits — it applies to all holders.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming the cosigner requirement only applies to younger permit holders.

---

**Q5. [Type: RECALL]** When is a cosigner required for a Provisional License?

**Answer:** Only when the provisional license holder is under 18.

**Explanation:** The cosigner requirement for provisional licenses only applies to drivers under 18. Once a driver turns 18, no cosigner is needed for their provisional license.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't assume all provisional license holders need a cosigner — only those under 18.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the cosigner requirement is the same as for Learner's Permits.

---

**Q6. [Type: RECALL]** Can a Learner's Permit holder use a cell phone while driving?

**Answer:** No — no cell phone use is allowed except for emergency 911 calls.

**Explanation:** Learner's Permit holders may not use a cell phone while driving under any circumstances except to make emergency 911 calls.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't confuse cell phone rules with texting rules — both are restricted, but the specifics differ by license stage.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking hands-free phone use is allowed for Learner's Permit holders.

---

**Q7. [Type: RECALL]** Can a provisional license holder under 18 use a hands-free phone device?

**Answer:** No — no cell phone use under the age of 18 except for emergency 911 calls.

**Explanation:** Drivers under 18 with a provisional license may not use any cell phone while driving, including hands-free devices. The hands-free exception only applies to provisional license holders 18 and over.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't think hands-free is allowed for all provisional holders — only those 18+.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the under-18 and over-18 rules for cell phone use.

---

**Q8. [Type: RECALL]** Is texting allowed at any license stage in Maryland?

**Answer:** No texting except for emergency 911 texts — this applies to all three license stages.

**Explanation:** Maryland's texting ban is absolute across all license types. The only exception is for emergency 911 texts.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't assume texting becomes legal at some point — it never does (except 911).

**Common Mistake:** Thinking that a full license permits texting while driving.

---

**Q9. [Type: RECALL]** What are the seat belt requirements across all license stages?

**Answer:** Driver and all passengers must wear seat belts or be in age-appropriate car seats/boosters — identical for all three stages.

**Explanation:** Seat belt requirements do not change between license stages. Every stage requires the driver and all passengers to be properly restrained.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't assume seat belt rules change between stages — they are constant.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking seat belt requirements become less strict with a full license.

---

**Q10. [Type: RECALL]** What is the nighttime restriction for provisional license holders under 18?

**Answer:** No driving between 12:00 AM and 5:00 AM, except for school, sports, job, or volunteer work.

**Explanation:** The nighttime curfew runs from midnight to 5 AM. Exceptions exist for essential activities like school, employment, sports, and volunteering.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't forget the exceptions — nighttime driving is not absolutely prohibited.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking no nighttime driving is allowed at all for provisional holders under 18.

---

**Q11. [Type: APPLICATION]** A 17-year-old provisional license holder is driving to a friend's house at 1 AM. Is this legal?

**Answer:** No — the nighttime restriction prohibits driving between 12 AM and 5 AM unless for school, sports, job, or volunteer work. Driving to a friend's house does not qualify for an exception.

**Explanation:** The curfew applies from midnight to 5 AM, and driving to a friend's house is not one of the approved exceptions. The driver would be in violation.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't think all nighttime driving is prohibited — exceptions exist for essential activities.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing the specific exceptions to the nighttime curfew.

---

**Q12. [Type: APPLICATION]** A 16-year-old provisional license holder received their license 4 months ago and wants to drive 3 friends to school. Is this legal?

**Answer:** No — for the first 151 days (approximately 5 months), they cannot carry non-family passengers. Four months is within the restriction period.

**Explanation:** The passenger restriction applies for the first 151 days. Four months is about 120 days, which is within the restriction. Friends are not immediate family, so they cannot be passengers.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't assume 4 months is past the restriction period — 151 days is about 5 months.

**Common Mistake:** Not calculating whether 151 days have passed since license issuance.

---

**Q13. [Type: APPLICATION]** A provisional license holder who is 19 years old is pulled over while texting. Will they receive a citation?

**Answer:** Yes — texting is banned at all license stages, regardless of age. The only exception is emergency 911 texts.

**Explanation:** Even though this driver is over 18 and has a provisional license, the texting ban applies to all drivers. Age does not provide an exception for texting.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't assume being over 18 changes the texting rules — it does not.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the age-based cell phone exceptions with the universal texting ban.

---

**Q14. [Type: APPLICATION]** What happens on a provisional license holder's third offense?

**Answer:** 180 days suspension or revocation, 180 days of work/school restriction, and must attend Young Driver Improvement Program.

**Explanation:** The third offense carries the most severe consequences before full revocation: a 180-day suspension, 180-day restriction to work/school only, and mandatory completion of the Young Driver Improvement Program.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't confuse third offense with fourth (180-day revocation). Suspension is temporary; revocation is permanent.

**Common Mistake:** Mixing up the consequences for different offense levels.

---

**Q15. [Type: APPLICATION]** At how many points does a Maryland driver face license suspension?

**Answer:** 8 points.

**Explanation:** Maryland's point system triggers license suspension at 8 points, with possible additional penalties. This is separate from the offense-based consequences.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't confuse point-based suspension (8 points) with offense-based consequences (which escalate differently).

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the point thresholds: 5-7 = DIP, 8 = suspension, 12 = revocation.

---

**Q16. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** How do the three license stages differ in terms of cell phone restrictions?

**Answer:** Learner's Permit: no cell phone use (911 only). Provisional: no use under 18 (911 only), hands-free allowed 18+. Full License: hands-free allowed.

**Explanation:** Cell phone restrictions progressively loosen: no use at all for Learner's Permit, no use under 18 but hands-free at 18+ for Provisional, and hands-free for full License. Texting remains banned at all stages.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't think cell phone rules are the same across all stages — they change with each stage.

**Common Mistake:** Not recognizing the age-based exception within the Provisional stage.

---

**Q17. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Compare the cosigner requirements across all three license stages.

**Answer:** Learner's Permit: always required. Provisional: required only if under 18. Full License: not required.

**Explanation:** The cosigner requirement decreases as the driver progresses through the stages. It is universal for Learner's Permits, age-dependent for Provisional Licenses, and nonexistent for full Licenses.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't assume the cosigner requirement is the same for all stages.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing that the cosigner requirement changes based on both license type and age.

---

**Q18. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Why does Maryland's texting ban apply to all license stages while cell phone restrictions vary?

**Answer:** Texting is considered universally dangerous regardless of experience level, while cell phone use risks can be partially mitigated with hands-free technology for more experienced drivers.

**Explanation:** Texting requires visual attention away from the road, making it inherently dangerous for all drivers. Cell phone use with hands-free technology reduces but does not eliminate risk, so Maryland allows it for more experienced drivers (18+ provisional, full license).

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't assume the two restrictions follow the same logic — they have different rationales.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding why texting is treated more strictly than cell phone use.

---

**Q19. [Type: RECALL]** What happens on a fourth offense for provisional license holders?

**Answer:** 180-day revocation of the provisional license.

**Explanation:** The fourth offense results in the most severe consequence: a 180-day revocation, meaning the license is taken away entirely for 180 days.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't confuse revocation (permanent loss for a period) with suspension (temporary hold).

**Common Mistake:** Mixing up third and fourth offense consequences.

---

**Q20. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Explain how the Graduated Licensing System progressively grants more freedom at each stage.

**Answer:** Learner's Permit: maximum restrictions (no phone, cosigner required). Provisional: reduced restrictions (hands-free at 18+, nighttime curfew under 18). Full License: minimal restrictions (no texting, seat belts required).

**Explanation:** Each stage removes some restrictions while maintaining safety-critical rules. The Learner's Permit is the most restricted, the Provisional License adds independence with some limits, and the full License provides near-complete freedom with only the most essential safety rules remaining.

**MVA Test Trap:** Don't think the stages are just about age — each stage has specific rule changes.

**Common Mistake:** Not understanding the progression of freedom across the three stages.

## Quick Reference

- Minimum ages: 15y9m (permit), 16y6m (provisional), 18 (full)
- Cosigner: always (permit), under 18 (provisional), none (full)
- Seat belts: required at all stages
- Cell phone: no use (permit), no under 18 / hands-free 18+ (provisional), hands-free (full)
- Texting: banned at all stages (911 only)
- Nighttime curfew: 12am-5am (provisional, under 18)
- Passenger restriction: 151 days, no non-family (provisional, under 18)
- Alcohol: zero tolerance (permit & provisional), .07 DWI / .08 DUI (21+ full)
- 1st offense: DIP class
- 2nd: 30-day suspension + 90-day restriction
- 3rd: 180-day suspension + YDIP
- 4th: 180-day revocation
- Points: 5-7 DIP, 8 suspension, 12 revocation

**Test Day Tip:** When answering license comparison questions, create a quick mental table: Permit | Provisional | Full License. Fill in each restriction row by row. This prevents mixing up which rules apply to which stage.
