## [1:30] Section 1: Minimum Ages and Cosigner Requirements

Let us start with the basics. Maryland has three stages in its Graduated Licensing System. Each stage has a different minimum age.

[PAUSE]

The **Learner's Permit** is available at fifteen years and nine months. The **Provisional License** is available at sixteen years and six months. The **Full Driver License** is available at eighteen years.

[PAUSE]

Memory trick. Think of the ages in order. Fifteen-nineteen, sixteen-six, eighteen. The numbers get bigger as the license gets more complete.

[PAUSE]

Now, cosigner requirements. For the Learner's Permit, you **must** have a cosigner who can request MVA cancel the permit for any reason. This applies to all permit holders.

[PAUSE]

For the Provisional License, a cosigner is required only if you are **under eighteen**. The cosigner can request MVA cancel the license for any reason. Once you turn eighteen, no cosigner is needed.

[PAUSE]

For the Full Driver License, there is **no cosigner** required at all.

[PAUSE]

Here is what the MVA exam might ask. They may ask which license type requires a cosigner for all holders. The answer is the Learner's Permit. For the Provisional License, it depends on age.

## [4:30] Section 2: Phone and Texting Rules

This is where the MVA exam really tests your knowledge. Let us break it down by license stage.

[PAUSE]

**Cell phone use.** Learner's Permit: no cell phone use except for emergency 911 calls. Provisional License: no cell phone use if under eighteen except 911. But if you are eighteen or older with a provisional license, you may use a **hands-free** device. Full Driver License: hands-free device is allowed.

[PAUSE]

**Texting.** This is simpler. Texting is banned at **all three stages**. The only exception is emergency 911 texts. This rule never changes.

[PAUSE]

Here is a key distinction the MVA exam loves to test. Cell phone rules have an age-based exception within the Provisional stage. If you are under eighteen, no cell phone at all. If you are eighteen or older, hands-free is okay. But texting rules have **no age-based exception**. No texting, period, except 911.

[PAUSE]

Memory trick. Think "no text ever." Texting is always banned. Cell phone rules vary by age and stage, but texting is always off limits.

## [7:30] Section 3: Nighttime and Passenger Restrictions

These restrictions only apply to **provisional license holders under eighteen**. This is important to remember.

[PAUSE]

**Nighttime restriction.** If you are under eighteen with a provisional license, you may not drive between **midnight and 5 AM**. Exceptions exist for school, sports, job, or volunteer work. If you are eighteen or older, or if you have a full license, there are no nighttime restrictions.

[PAUSE]

**Passenger restriction.** If you are under eighteen with a provisional license, for the **first one hundred fifty-one days**, you may not carry passengers other than immediate family members. After one hundred fifty-one days, or once you turn eighteen, there are no passenger restrictions.

[PAUSE]

Here is what the MVA exam might ask. They may present a scenario where a seventeen-year-old is driving at 1 AM. Is this legal? The answer depends on the purpose. If driving home from work, the exception applies. If driving to a friend's house, it is a violation.

[PAUSE]

Another test question might ask about the passenger restriction period. It is one hundred fifty-one days, not five months, not six months, not the entire provisional period. One hundred fifty-one days.

## [10:00] Section 4: Consequences and Point System

Let us cover the consequences for violations and the point system.

[PAUSE]

For provisional license holders under eighteen, consequences escalate with each offense. First offense: DIP class. Second offense: thirty-day suspension plus ninety-day work and school restriction. Third offense: one hundred eighty-day suspension or revocation, one hundred eighty-day restriction, and you must attend the Young Driver Improvement Program. Fourth offense: one hundred eighty-day revocation.

[PAUSE]

For the point system, which applies to all license holders. Five to seven points: DIP class required. Eight points: license suspension with possible additional penalties. Twelve points: license revocation with possible additional penalties.

[PAUSE]

Memory trick. Think "five-seven-eight-twelve." Five to seven gets you into DIP class. Eight gets your license suspended. Twelve gets your license revoked. The numbers escalate, and so do the consequences.

## [11:30] Quick Review

Let us do a rapid recap of the entire license comparison chart.

[PAUSE]

Minimum ages: fifteen years nine months, sixteen years six months, eighteen. Cosigner: always for permit, under eighteen for provisional, none for full. Seat belts: required at all stages. Cell phone: no use for permit, no under eighteen and hands-free at eighteen plus for provisional, hands-free for full. Texting: banned at all stages. Nighttime curfew: midnight to five AM for provisional under eighteen. Passenger restriction: one hundred fifty-one days for provisional under eighteen. Alcohol: zero tolerance for permit and provisional, point zero seven DWI and point zero eight DUI for full license holders twenty-one plus. Point thresholds: five to seven DIP, eight suspension, twelve revocation.

[PAUSE]
