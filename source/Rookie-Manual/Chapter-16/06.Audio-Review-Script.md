# Chapter 16 - Audio Review Script

## [0:00] Introduction

Welcome to your audio review for Maryland Driver's Manual chapter sixteen, covering **night driving** and **driving in adverse weather conditions**. [PAUSE]

In this twelve-minute session, we will cover everything you need to know about staying safe when the sun goes down, when the rain starts falling, and when fog or snow rolls in. [PAUSE]

These topics show up frequently on the Maryland knowledge test, and more importantly, they could save your life on the road. [LONG PAUSE]

## [1:30] Section 1: Night Driving Essentials

Let us start with nighttime driving. The Maryland Driver's Manual is very clear about what you need to do **before** you even start the car. [PAUSE]

First, check **all** your exterior lights. That means front lights, rear lights, brake lights, turn signals, and both your high and low beams. Not just one or two — all of them. [PAUSE]

Dirty windows and headlights are a bigger deal than you might think. Dirty windows add to glare and make it harder to see. Dirty headlights can reduce their efficiency significantly. So clean both the inside and outside of your windows, and make sure your headlight lenses are clear. [PAUSE]

Now here is a critical rule for the MVA exam. When you are driving at night and you see the headlights of an oncoming vehicle, you must **switch to low beams**. [EMPHASIS: switch] High beams pointed at an oncoming driver can temporarily blind them. And the same applies in fog. [PAUSE]

In fog, you **always** use low beams. Here is why. High beams angle upward and the light bounces off the fog droplets right back into your eyes. Low beams stay lower and cut through the fog instead of reflecting off it. [PAUSE]

The MVA exam loves to ask about fog. Remember this rule. Fog equals low beams. Always. [LONG PAUSE]

## [4:00] Section 2: Adjusting to Night Conditions

Once you are on the road at night, you need to make several adjustments. [PAUSE]

Adjust your **rear-view mirror** to the night setting. Most cars have a day-night toggle that tilts the mirror to reduce glare from headlights behind you. Use it every time you drive after dark. [PAUSE]

Never use your interior lights while driving. The light reflects off the windshield and creates glare that makes it nearly impossible to see outside. If you need to check something, pull over safely first. [PAUSE]

Keep your eyes moving constantly. Watch for flashes of light at the tops of hills, around road bends, and at intersections. Those flashes tell you another vehicle is approaching even before you can see it. [PAUSE]

**Increase your following distance** at night. You have less time to detect hazards, so you need more space to react. [PAUSE]

And do not forget about fatigue. Night driving requires intense concentration and it is tiring. The manual says to take frequent breaks to give your eyes and mind a rest. If you feel drowsy, pull over. Do not push through it. [PAUSE]

Remember, Maryland requires a minimum of **ten hours** of supervised nighttime driving before you can get your provisional license. That is not just a recommendation. It is part of the Graduated Licensing System. [LONG PAUSE]

## [7:00] Section 3: Wet Roads and Hydroplaning

Now let us talk about driving in the rain and on wet roads. [PAUSE]

Even in light rain, turn on your headlights. You need to see, and more importantly, you need to **be seen** by other drivers. [PAUSE]

Turn on your front and rear defoggers to keep your windows clear. Wet roads mean more moisture in the air, and your windows will fog up quickly. [PAUSE]

Here is the key rule. It takes **longer to stop** on wet roadways. So increase your following distance and drive at a slower pace. [PAUSE]

Brake **earlier** and **less forcefully** than you normally would. This does two things. It increases the stopping distance between you and the car ahead. And it signals to the driver behind you that you are slowing down. [PAUSE]

Watch for pedestrians. People get distracted opening umbrellas or rushing to get out of the rain. They may step into the road without looking. [PAUSE]

If you cannot see the road or the car in front of you, or if you are feeling anxious, pull over to a safe spot. No destination is worth risking a crash. [PAUSE]

Now let us talk about **hydroplaning**. This happens when water on the road is deeper than your tire tread and you are going too fast. The water gets between your tires and the road surface, and you lose all friction. That means you lose control. [PAUSE]

To avoid hydroplaning, avoid deep puddles. If you cannot avoid a puddle, slow down to minimize the risk. [LONG PAUSE]

## [10:00] Section 4: Fog, Snow, and Ice

In fog, remember our rule. Slow down, use low beams, and if your vehicle has fog lights, use them. [PAUSE]

Now for snow and ice, which are especially important here in Maryland where winter weather varies widely across the state. [PAUSE]

Before you even start driving, make sure your **entire vehicle** is free of snow and ice. That includes the roof. Snow flying off your car can obstruct the driver behind you. [PAUSE]

When driving on snow, steer smoothly, travel slowly, and brake gently. Remember, stopping distances are **ten times greater** on snow than on dry roads. [EMPHASIS: ten times] That is not an exaggeration. Ten times. [PAUSE]

Watch for icy conditions, especially in **shaded areas** and on **bridges and overpasses**. These spots freeze before the rest of the road because they lose heat faster. [PAUSE]

The MVA exam frequently asks where ice forms first. The answer is always bridges, overpasses, and shaded areas. [LONG PAUSE]

## [12:30] Quick Review

Let us do a rapid recap of the must-know facts. [PAUSE]

Fog means low beams. Always. [PAUSE]

Wet roads mean headlights on, increased following distance, and gentler braking. [PAUSE]

Hydroplaning is caused by water deeper than tire tread plus excessive speed. [PAUSE]

Snow increases stopping distance by ten times. [PAUSE]

Ice forms first on bridges, overpasses, and shaded areas. [PAUSE]

Night driving requires ten hours of supervised practice for your provisional license. [PAUSE]

Never use interior lights while driving. Pull over if you need light. [PAUSE]

And always take breaks to prevent fatigue when driving at night. [LONG PAUSE]

## [13:30] Closing

You have just reviewed two of the most important chapters in the Maryland Driver's Manual. Night driving and adverse weather are where experience matters most, and the MVA exam will test whether you know the correct adjustments. [PAUSE]

If any of these concepts feel unfamiliar, replay this audio. Spaced repetition is one of the most effective study techniques. Listen again tomorrow, then again in two days. [PAUSE]

You are building the knowledge to be a safe, confident driver. Keep going, and good luck on your Maryland knowledge test. [LONG PAUSE]
