# Chapter 16 — Master Memorization Sheet

## Chapter Summary
Night driving and wet road conditions require specific adjustments: proper use of headlights, increased following distance, awareness of glare, and fatigue management. Wet roads demand earlier braking and slower speeds to prevent hydroplaning.

## Why It Matters
More fatal crashes occur at night than during the day. New drivers are especially vulnerable because they lack experience judging distances and hazards in low visibility. Maryland's Graduated Licensing System requires **10 hours of supervised night driving** before obtaining a provisional license.

## High-Yield Facts
- Check ALL exterior lights before driving: front, rear, brake lights, turn signals, high and low beams
- Clean windows and headlights inside and out — dirty glass increases glare and reduces visibility
- Use **low beams** when oncoming headlights are visible or in foggy conditions
- Use your **day/night rear-view mirror** to reduce glare from headlights behind you
- **Never** use interior lights while driving — pull over safely first
- Keep your eyes moving; watch for light flashes at hilltops, curves, and intersections
- **Increase following distance** at night to allow extra reaction time
- Take frequent breaks to combat night driving fatigue
- On wet roads: headlights on even in light rain, use defoggers, brake earlier and less forcefully
- **Hydroplaning** = water deeper than tire tread + excessive speed = loss of friction and control
- In fog: use **low beams only** — high beams reflect back and worsen visibility
- On snow: stopping distances are **10 times greater** than on dry roads
- Watch for ice on **shaded areas, bridges, and overpasses** first
- Remove ALL snow and ice from your vehicle before driving

## MVA Test Traps
- **Trap:** "Use high beams in fog for better visibility" — WRONG. High beams reflect off fog particles and blind you.
- **Trap:** "Interior lights help you see at night" — WRONG. They cause glare on the windshield and reduce your ability to see outside.
- **Trap:** "Wet roads require harder braking to stop faster" — WRONG. Brake earlier and **less forcefully** to maintain control and signal drivers behind you.
- **Trap:** "Hydroplaning only happens in heavy rain" — WRONG. It can happen with any standing water combined with excessive speed.

## Common Mistakes
- Forgetting to clean headlights — dirty lenses can reduce output by 50%
- Using high beams when an oncoming car is approaching (within 500 feet)
- Not adjusting following distance in rain — the manual specifically says to increase it
- Not pulling over when visibility drops below safe levels
- Leaving snow/ice on the roof of the car — it can fly off and obstruct other drivers

## Memory Tricks
- **"Fog = Low"** — Fog always means low beams
- **"Rain = Brake Early, Brake Light"** — Earlier braking, less force
- **"10x on Snow"** — Stopping distance multiplies by 10 on snow
- **"Bridges freeze first"** — Bridges and overpasses ice before regular roads
- **"Lights, Mirrors, Eyes"** — The three things to manage at night: headlights, rear-view mirror, keep eyes moving

## Must-Know Signs and Signals
- No specific road signs covered in this chapter — focus is on vehicle equipment and driving behavior adjustments for darkness and adverse weather

## Maryland-Specific Laws
- **GDL Requirement:** Minimum 10 hours of supervised night driving required before provisional license
- **Headlight Law:** Headlights required from sunset to sunrise and anytime visibility is less than 1,000 feet
- **Following Distance:** Maryland recommends increased following distance in wet/night conditions
- **Vehicle Equipment:** All exterior lights must be operational before driving
