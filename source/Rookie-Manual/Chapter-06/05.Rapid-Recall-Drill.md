## Questions

**Q1. [Type: RECALL]** What are the five hazards to check for during a pre-entry vehicle inspection?

**Answer:** Broken glass on or around the vehicle, body damage to the vehicle, condition of tires and lights, fluid leaks under the vehicle, and people or objects near the vehicle.

**Explanation:** The pre-entry check is a 360-degree walk-around before every drive. Catching a flat tire, broken light, or fluid leak before driving prevents breakdowns and accidents.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students often forget "people or objects" and focus only on vehicle condition. The MVA tests whether you remember to check the ENTIRE environment around the car.

**Common Mistake:** Only checking the front and rear — you must check ALL sides of the vehicle.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Pre-entry Check section

---

**Q2. [Type: RECALL]** What is the correct hand position on the steering wheel according to the Maryland Driver's Manual?

**Answer:** Left hand at 9 o'clock and right hand at 3 o'clock.

**Explanation:** This position provides maximum steering control and keeps your hands away from the airbag deployment zone, reducing injury risk in a crash.

**MVA Test Trap:** Many students still answer "10 and 2" — that was the old recommendation. The 9 and 3 position is now standard because of airbag safety.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing the old "10 and 2" position with the current "9 and 3" recommendation.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Steering section

---

**Q3. [Type: RECALL]** In Maryland, who is required to wear a seat belt?

**Answer:** Everybody in the vehicle, no matter where they are seated or what their age.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires all occupants to wear seat belts. The driver can be ticketed if any person in the vehicle is not buckled up.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students sometimes think passengers over 16 or back-seat passengers are exempt. Maryland law covers ALL occupants.

**Common Mistake:** Believing that only the driver and front-seat passengers need seat belts.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Seat Belt section

---

**Q4. [Type: RECALL]** When backing up, where should you place your hand on the steering wheel?

**Answer:** At the 12 o'clock position (top of the wheel).

**Explanation:** Grasping the wheel at 12 o'clock with one hand allows you to steer effectively while your other hand can rest on the back of the passenger seat to support your turned body position.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may answer "both hands on the wheel" — but backing requires one hand at 12 o'clock and the other supporting your turned body.

**Common Mistake:** Using the same hand position as normal driving when backing up.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Backing section

---

**Q5. [Type: RECALL]** What is the proper body position when backing a vehicle?

**Answer:** Turn your body to the right with your arm over the back of the seat, looking through the rear window.

**Explanation:** This position lets you see directly behind the vehicle. Mirrors alone create blind spots — turning your body eliminates them.

**MVA Test Trap:** "Use your mirrors" is tempting, but mirrors only show part of the area behind you. The manual specifically warns against relying only on mirrors.

**Common Mistake:** Trying to back up by looking only in the rearview and side mirrors.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Backing section

**Q6. [Type: APPLICATION]** A new driver is sitting in the car for the first time. What should they do BEFORE starting the engine?

**Answer:** Adjust the seat, adjust the mirrors, fasten the seat belt, locate all controls (headlights, turn signal, brake, gas, etc.), and turn off electronic devices.

**Explanation:** All adjustments must be made before the vehicle moves. Starting the engine without being properly positioned creates a safety hazard.

**MVA Test Trap:** "Start the engine and then adjust" — adjustments must happen BEFORE starting the engine.

**Common Mistake:** Adjusting mirrors after the car is already in motion.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Getting Ready to Drive section

---

**Q7. [Type: APPLICATION]** During a practice session, the new driver says they can feel the car pulling slightly. The coach notices the front right tire looks low. What should happen next?

**Answer:** The practice session should be stopped immediately. The tire should be inspected and inflated or replaced before driving further.

**Explanation:** The pre-entry check is supposed to catch tire condition issues. If a low tire is discovered during driving, it's dangerous to continue. This is why the pre-entry walk-around matters.

**MVA Test Trap:** "Continue driving carefully" — never continue with a visibly low tire. It can blow out.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming a slightly low tire is not a big deal. Even a small reduction in pressure is dangerous.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Pre-entry Check — condition of tires

**Q8. [Type: APPLICATION]** The new driver starts the car but forgets to adjust the mirrors until they're already moving in the parking lot. Why is this a problem?

**Answer:** Adjusting mirrors while driving takes your eyes and attention away from the road. You may not see a pedestrian, another vehicle, or an obstacle.

**Explanation:** All adjustments — seat, mirrors, and seat belt — must happen before the vehicle moves. Multitasking while driving, even in a parking lot, is dangerous.

**MVA Test Trap:** "A parking lot is safe enough to adjust" — even parking lots have pedestrians, shopping carts, and other vehicles.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking low-speed environments are safe enough to multitask.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Getting Ready to Drive section

---

**Q9. [Type: APPLICATION]** A student is practicing backing in an empty lot. They press the gas pedal to move backward. The coach tells them to stop. Why?

**Answer:** You should never use the gas pedal when backing up. Allow the vehicle to move at idle speed — a slow walking pace.

**Explanation:** Using the gas when backing gives you less control and more speed. Backing requires precision and slow movement to watch for pedestrians, bicyclists, and obstacles.

**MVA Test Trap:** "A little gas is fine in an empty lot" — the manual is absolute: do not accelerate using the gas pedal when backing.

**Common Mistake:** Applying gas when backing because it feels natural to press the accelerator to move.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Backing section

---

**Q10. [Type: APPLICATION]** Before the new driver attempts to back up, they should do a visual search around the vehicle. What exactly are they looking for?

**Answer:** Pedestrians, bicyclists, other vehicles, animals, and any obstacles in the path behind and around the vehicle.

**Explanation:** Backing crashes are among the most common low-speed accidents. Children, pets, and pedestrians are especially hard to see behind a vehicle. The search must be thorough before the car moves.

**MVA Test Trap:** "Just look for other cars" — the manual specifically lists pedestrians, bicyclists, vehicles, AND animals.

**Common Mistake:** Only checking directly behind the car instead of the full area around it.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Backing section

---

**Q11. [Type: APPLICATION]** The coach asks the new driver to describe what they see while driving through the parking lot. What is the purpose of this exercise?

**Answer:** It helps the coach correct mistakes before they happen and ensures the new driver is aware of their surroundings.

**Explanation:** Having the new driver verbalize their observations — what they see, what they're doing, and what they're thinking — builds situational awareness, which is more important than any single driving skill.

**MVA Test Trap:** "It's just a coaching exercise" — it's actually a core safety skill. Awareness of surroundings is the foundation of defensive driving.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking driving skill is about car control alone; awareness is equally critical.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Coach's Tip section

---

**Q12. [Type: APPLICATION]** A new driver completes the pre-entry check and finds a small puddle under the car. They aren't sure what it is. Should they drive?

**Answer:** No. Fluid leaks can indicate serious problems — brake fluid, coolant, or transmission fluid leaks can lead to brake failure or engine damage. The vehicle should be inspected before driving.

**Explanation:** The pre-entry check specifically includes looking for fluid leaks. Any unknown fluid under the vehicle warrants investigation before proceeding.

**MVA Test Trap:** "It's probably just water from the A/C" — you can't assume. The manual lists fluid leaks as a specific hazard to check.

**Common Mistake:** Dismissing small puddles as condensation without investigation.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Pre-entry Check section

**Q13. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Compare the steering technique used during normal driving versus backing up. What are the key differences?

**Answer:** Normal driving uses hands at 9 and 3 for balanced control. Backing uses one hand at 12 o'clock while the other arm rests over the back of the seat to support a turned body position looking rearward.

**Explanation:** Different driving maneuvers require different techniques. Normal driving prioritizes control and airbag safety (9 and 3). Backing prioritizes rearward visibility, requiring a body turn and different hand placement.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the same hand position applies to all driving situations. The manual teaches different positions for different tasks.

**Common Mistake:** Using the 9 and 3 position when backing, which prevents proper rearward visibility.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Steering and Backing sections

---

**Q14. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Why does the Maryland Driver's Manual require ALL vehicle occupants to wear seat belts, not just the driver and front passengers?

**Answer:** In a crash, unbelted passengers become projectiles that can injure or kill belted occupants. Maryland law recognizes that seat belt use is a vehicle-wide safety requirement.

**Explanation:** An unbelted rear-seat passenger in a 30 mph crash can generate forces equivalent to being hit by a small car. Seat belts protect both the wearer and everyone else in the vehicle.

**MVA Test Trap:** "Back-seat passengers are safer because they're farther from the front" — actually, unbelted rear passengers are extremely dangerous to everyone.

**Common Mistake:** Believing rear-seat passengers don't need belts because they're "farther from the impact."

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Seat Belt section

**Q15. [Type: RECALL]** Name four vehicle controls a new driver must locate before driving.

**Answer:** Headlights, turn signal, brake pedal, and gas pedal. (Also acceptable: hazard lights, horn, heater/defroster/A/C, parking brake, windshield wipers, door locks, gear shift.)

**Explanation:** A driver who doesn't know where these controls are cannot operate the vehicle safely. In an emergency, fumbling for headlights or the brake is dangerous.

**MVA Test Trap:** Students may list only "brake and gas" and forget secondary controls like headlights and turn signals.

**Common Mistake:** Focusing only on brake and gas while ignoring safety-critical controls like headlights and turn signals.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Getting Ready to Drive section

---

**Q16. [Type: APPLICATION]** A new driver is about to practice turns in a parking lot. They signal, check traffic, and begin turning the wheel sharply. The coach corrects them. Why?

**Answer:** The steering wheel should be turned smoothly, not sharply. Sharp turns can cause loss of control, especially for new drivers who haven't developed a feel for the vehicle.

**Explanation:** The manual specifically states to "turn the steering wheel smoothly." Jerky or sharp steering inputs can destabilize the vehicle and cause skidding.

**MVA Test Trap:** "In a parking lot, sharp turns are fine because you're going slow" — the manual teaches smooth steering as a universal habit.

**Common Mistake:** Turning the wheel too aggressively because of nervousness or inexperience.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Turns section

---

**Q17. [Type: RECALL]** What should a new driver do with electronic devices before beginning a practice driving session?

**Answer:** Turn off all electronic devices.

**Explanation:** Electronic devices are a major distraction. Maryland law prohibits hand-held phone use while driving, and for practice sessions, all devices should be off to maintain full focus.

**MVA Test Trap:** "Put them on silent" is not enough — the manual says turn them OFF.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking silent mode or putting the phone face-down is sufficient.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Getting Ready to Drive section

---

**Q18. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** How does the pre-entry check relate to the seat belt requirement? Why are both part of the same "getting ready" process?

**Answer:** Both are proactive safety steps taken BEFORE the vehicle moves. The pre-entry check ensures the vehicle is safe to drive; seat belts ensure occupants are protected if something goes wrong despite those checks.

**Explanation:** Safe driving requires preparation on two fronts: vehicle readiness (pre-entry check) and occupant protection (seat belts). Neither alone is sufficient — you need both every time you drive.

**MVA Test Trap:** Treating seat belts and pre-entry checks as separate, unrelated topics. They're both part of the same pre-drive safety sequence.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking the pre-entry check is optional if the car "looks fine."

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Pre-entry Check and Seat Belt sections

---

**Q19. [Type: RECALL]** How should a new driver practice the brake and gas pedals before actually driving?

**Answer:** Engage the brake and gas alternately several times while stationary to get the feel of both pedals before moving the vehicle.

**Explanation:** New drivers often confuse brake and gas or press the wrong pedal. Practicing the feel of both pedals while stopped builds muscle memory and reduces the risk of pedal confusion.

**MVA Test Trap:** "Just start driving and you'll get the feel" — the manual specifically recommends practicing pedal feel before moving.

**Common Mistake:** Skipping the pedal familiarization step and going straight to driving.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Moving the Vehicle section

---

**Q20. [Type: APPLICATION]** The new driver has completed all pre-entry checks, adjusted everything, and feels ready to drive. What is the very first thing they should do to begin moving the vehicle?

**Answer:** Start the vehicle with their foot on the brake, then shift into Drive and slowly release the brake.

**Explanation:** The brake must be depressed when starting the engine (safety standard). Once started, shifting to Drive and slowly releasing the brake allows the car to begin moving gently.

**MVA Test Trap:** "Shift to Drive and press the gas" — the manual says slowly release the brake first. No gas is needed to begin moving.

**Common Mistake:** Pressing the gas immediately after shifting to Drive instead of letting the brake release allow gentle movement.

**Reference:** Driver's Manual Chapter 6, Moving the Vehicle section

## Quick Reference

- **Pre-entry check:** Broken glass, body damage, tires/lights, fluid leaks, people/objects
- **Controls to know:** Headlights, turn signal, hazards, horn, heater/defroster, parking brake, wipers, door locks, gear shift, gas, brake
- **Seat belt law:** EVERY person in the vehicle, all ages, all seats
- **Steering position:** 9 and 3 (not 10 and 2)
- **Backing hand position:** 12 o'clock
- **Backing speed:** Idle creep only, no gas pedal
- **Backing body position:** Turn right, arm over seat back, look through rear window
- **All adjustments BEFORE moving the vehicle**
- **Turn off ALL electronic devices before practicing**

**Test Day Tip:** When you see a seat belt question on the MVA exam, remember: "Everyone, everywhere, every time." Maryland has NO exemptions for age or seating position.
