## Questions

**Q1. [Type: RECALL]** Do pedestrians have the right-of-way at unmarked crosswalks?

**Answer:** Yes. Pedestrians have the right-of-way at both marked and unmarked crosswalks. Most intersections have a crosswalk even if it is not marked.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires drivers to stop for pedestrians in any crosswalk, whether painted on the pavement or not. The crosswalk exists at most intersections by default.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you only need to stop at crosswalks with painted lines — unmarked crosswalks carry the same legal requirement.

**Common Mistake:** Driving through intersections without checking for pedestrians because there are no painted crosswalk lines.

---

**Q2. [Type: RECALL]** What is the minimum stopping distance from a school bus with flashing red lights?

**Answer:** At least 20 feet from the back of the bus if you are behind it, or at least 20 feet from the front if you are in front of it.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires a 20-foot buffer in either direction. You may not start moving again until the bus starts moving or turns off its flashing red lights.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing 20 feet with 15 feet or other distances — the exact number is tested.

**Common Mistake:** Starting to drive as soon as the bus stops flashing, rather than waiting for the bus to move or lights to turn off.

---

**Q3. [Type: RECALL]** What are the four truck No-Zones?

**Answer:** Side No-Zones, Rear No-Zone, Front No-Zone, and Backing Up No-Zone. These are blind spots where the truck driver cannot see your vehicle.

**Explanation:** The side blind spots extend along both sides of the truck. The rear No-Zone is directly behind the truck. The front No-Zone is immediately in front. The backing up No-Zone is behind a truck that is reversing.

**MVA Test Trap:** Forgetting that the right-side blind spot runs the length of the trailer and extends out three lanes.

**Common Mistake:** Thinking trucks only have blind spots on the sides — all four sides have significant blind areas.

---

**Q4. [Type: RECALL]** What should you do when you see an emergency vehicle approaching with sirens and flashing lights?

**Answer:** Immediately move as close as possible to the edge of the road, stay out of intersections, and remain stopped until the emergency vehicle has passed.

**Explanation:** This is a three-part legal requirement. Pulling over, avoiding intersections, and waiting are all mandatory.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you can proceed through an intersection if you're already in it — you must stay out of intersections.

**Common Mistake:** Moving over but not remaining stopped until the vehicle has fully passed.

---

**Q5. [Type: RECALL]** Can you pass a school bus on a multi-lane road divided by a physical barrier?

**Answer:** If the bus is on the opposite side of the road divided by a physical barrier, you do not need to stop. You must stop only if the bus is on your side of the road.

**Explanation:** The physical barrier separation is the key factor. If there is a median or barrier dividing the lanes, traffic on the opposite side does not need to stop.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you must always stop for school buses regardless of road type — the barrier exception is frequently tested.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing that a physical barrier (not just a painted line) is required for the exception to apply.

---

**Q6. [Type: RECALL]** What is the minimum distance you must leave when passing a bicycle?

**Answer:** At least 3 feet between your vehicle and the bicycle.

**Explanation:** Maryland law requires a 3-foot buffer when passing a bicycle in a bike lane, on the shoulder, or in the same lane. Make sure you see the bicycle in your rear-view mirror before returning to your lane.

**MVA Test Trap:** Confusing 3 feet with 4 feet or 5 feet — the exact distance is tested.

**Common Mistake:** Not leaving enough space when passing on narrow roads or when the bicycle is in a bike lane.

---

**Q7. [Type: RECALL]** What is the legal status of bicycles on Maryland roads?

**Answer:** Bicycles are vehicles according to Maryland law. Bicyclists are allowed to use the road and must obey all traffic signals.

**Explanation:** This is a fundamental legal concept. Bicycles have the same rights and responsibilities as motor vehicles on the road.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking bicycles are "pedestrians on wheels" — they are legally vehicles, not pedestrians.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming bicyclists must stay on the shoulder or bike lane — they may use the full lane.

**Q8. [Type: RECALL]** How far can a fully loaded truck take to stop compared to a car?

**Answer:** A fully loaded truck can weigh more than 80,000 pounds and take the length of a football field to stop. Most cars weigh only about 2,000 pounds.

**Explanation:** This enormous difference in weight and stopping distance is why you should never cut in front of a truck. The truck simply cannot stop as quickly as your car.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking trucks stop about the same as cars — the football field comparison is a key MVA fact.

**Common Mistake:** Underestimating how much space a truck needs to stop safely.

**Q9. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're approaching an intersection and see a pedestrian stepping into an unmarked crosswalk while you have a green light. What should you do?

**Answer:** Yield to the pedestrian and allow them to cross. Even with a green light, you must yield to pedestrians in crosswalks.

**Explanation:** A green light gives you the right to proceed, but pedestrians always have the right-of-way in crosswalks. You must stop and let them cross.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking a green light means you have absolute right-of-way — pedestrians always win at crosswalks.

**Common Mistaking:** Speeding up to "beat" the pedestrian through the intersection.

---

**Q10. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're driving on a highway and notice a large truck signaling to change into your lane. You're in the truck's side blind spot. What should you do?

**Answer:** Immediately accelerate or decelerate to move out of the blind spot. Do not stay alongside the truck. If you cannot see the driver's face in their side-view mirror, they cannot see you.

**Explanation:** The truck driver may not know you are there. Moving out of the blind spot quickly is the safest action.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the truck will see you if you're close enough — if you can't see their mirror, they can't see you.

**Common Mistake:** Honking your horn and expecting the truck to react — the driver literally cannot see you.

---

**Q11. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're behind a motorcycle and notice the road has gravel ahead. The motorcycle slows down but its brake lights don't come on. What should you do?

**Answer:** Slow down and increase your following distance. Motorcyclists often reduce speed without using brakes, especially for road hazards like gravel.

**Explanation:** Motorcycles can decelerate without brake lights activating. Always maintain a 3-4 second following distance and watch for hazards that affect motorcycles more than cars.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking brake lights always indicate braking — motorcyclists can slow without them.

**Common Mistake:** Following too closely because you don't see brake lights.

---

**Q12. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're approaching an intersection and see a bicyclist ahead using hand signals. What does the signal mean, and what should you do?

**Answer:** Bicyclists use hand and arm signals because bicycles don't have turn signals. If a bicyclist extends their left arm straight out, they're turning left. A left arm bent upward means they're turning right. A left arm bent downward means they're stopping. You should yield to the bicyclist and give them space.

**Explanation:** Knowing bicyclist hand signals is important for predicting their movements and driving safely around them.

**MVA Test Trap:** Not knowing that bicyclists use hand signals instead of electronic signals.

**Common Mistake:** Ignoring bicyclist hand signals because you don't recognize them.

---

**Q13. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're driving at night and see a dim reflective object in your lane. What should you do?

**Answer:** Do not assume it is off the road. It could be a bicycle in a travel lane. Use your low beam headlights and be prepared to slow down or move over.

**Explanation:** Bicyclists sometimes ride in travel lanes at night to avoid road hazards on shoulders. Dim reflective objects may be bicycles.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking the reflective object is road debris — it could be a bicyclist.

**Common Mistake:** Swerving toward the object instead of carefully moving around it.

---

**Q14. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're parked on the side of the road. Before opening your car door, what should you check for?

**Answer:** Check for bicyclists who may be approaching from behind. Opening a door into a bicyclist's path is dangerous and can cause a serious crash.

**Explanation:** This is called "dooring" and it's a common cause of bicycle crashes. Always check your mirrors and look over your shoulder before opening the door.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking you only need to check for other cars — bicyclists are a critical check.

**Common Mistake:** Opening the door without looking, especially on roads without bike lanes.

---

**Q15. [Type: APPLICATION]** You're approaching a school bus that has stopped with flashing red lights. You're on a multi-lane road with a center turn lane (no physical barrier). Must you stop?

**Answer:** Yes. A center turn lane is not a physical barrier. You must stop at least 20 feet from the bus unless there is a physical barrier separating you from the bus.

**Explanation:** Only a physical barrier (like a concrete median) exempts you from stopping. Painted lines and turn lanes do not count as physical barriers.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking a center turn lane counts as a physical barrier — it does not.

**Common Mistake:** Confusing painted medians with physical barriers.

**Q16. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Compare the right-of-way rules for pedestrians, bicyclists, and motorcycles. How do they differ?

**Answer:** Pedestrians have right-of-way at crosswalks (marked and unmarked) and must be yielded to when turning. Bicyclists have right-of-way when a motor vehicle is turning, and when riding in bike lanes or shoulders — they are treated as vehicles. Motorcyclists have the same rights as any driver but require extra space and attention due to their vulnerability. The key difference is that pedestrians win at crosswalks, bicyclists win at turns and in bike lanes, and motorcyclists have full lane rights.

**Explanation:** All three groups have legal protections, but the specific scenarios differ. Pedestrians are protected at crossings, bicyclists at turns and in their lanes, and motorcyclists with full lane usage and following distance requirements.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking all vulnerable road users have the same right-of-way rules — each has specific scenarios.

**Common Mistake:** Applying pedestrian rules to bicyclists or vice versa.

**Q17. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** Why does the manual emphasize looking twice for motorcycles, and how does this relate to the driver fault statistics?

**Answer:** Motorcycles are smaller and harder to see than cars. They can be hidden in blind spots or missed in a quick look. The manual states that drivers are at fault in over half of car-motorcycle crashes. Looking twice — using mirrors and looking over your shoulder — directly addresses the visibility problem that leads to these at-fault crashes.

**Explanation:** The combination of motorcycle size (harder to see) and driver fault statistics (over 50% at fault) creates a compelling case for extra vigilance. The "look twice" rule is Maryland's answer to this problem.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking motorcycles are always at fault in crashes — drivers are at fault more than half the time.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming one quick look is sufficient before changing lanes.

**Q18. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** How do truck No-Zones compare to car blind spots, and why are they more dangerous?

**Answer:** Cars have blind spots on the sides and rear, but trucks have four large No-Zones: side, rear, front, and backing up. The truck's right-side blind spot alone extends the length of the trailer and out three lanes. Trucks need almost twice as much stopping distance as cars and can weigh 80,000+ pounds versus a car's 2,000 pounds. This means if you're in a truck's No-Zone and the truck makes a move, the consequences are far more severe than a car-to-car blind spot incident.

**Explanation:** Truck No-Zones are larger, more numerous, and more dangerous due to the truck's size, weight, and limited maneuverability. The combination of bigger blind spots and longer stopping distances makes truck interactions uniquely hazardous.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking truck blind spots are similar to car blind spots — they are significantly larger and more dangerous.

**Common Mistake:** Underestimating the size of truck blind spots, especially the right side extending three lanes.

**Q19. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** What are the key differences between how Maryland law treats pedestrians, bicyclists, and mopeds/scooters regarding road access?

**Answer:** Pedestrians use crosswalks and sidewalks. Bicycles are vehicles that can use all roads except interstate highways and tolled bridges/tunnels. Mopeds and scooters can use any road posted at 50 mph or less. Each has different infrastructure: pedestrians use crosswalks, bicycles can use full lanes or bike lanes, and mopeds ride on the road or shoulder as close to the right as safely possible.

**Explanation:** Maryland law creates a hierarchy of road access based on the vehicle type and its capabilities. Each has specific rights and limitations that drivers must understand.

**MVA Test Trap:** Assuming all three have the same road access — each has different rules.

**Common Mistake:** Not knowing that mopeds are limited to 50 mph roads.

---

**Q20. [Type: SYNTHESIS]** How does ADAS technology relate to the driver's responsibility, and what are its key limitations?

**Answer:** ADAS (Advanced Driver Assistance Systems) includes features like Automatic Emergency Braking, Lane Keeping Assistance, and Blind Spot Warning. When used properly, they prevent 40% of crashes. However, the driver is ALWAYS responsible for safe operation. ADAS has limitations in rain, snow, fog, on hills, and around curves. Technology names vary by vehicle and don't always describe what it does. You must keep sensors clean, software updated, and be familiar with your specific vehicle's systems.

**Explanation:** ADAS is a tool that assists the driver, not a replacement for driver attention. The manual explicitly warns against over-reliance and emphasizes that driver awareness through mirrors and cameras remains essential.

**MVA Test Trap:** Thinking ADAS means you can relax your attention — the driver is always responsible.

**Common Mistake:** Assuming ADAS works perfectly in all conditions — it has significant limitations.
